\title{Hierarchical Tactical Corridor Planning and Control\\
for Wheel-to-Wheel Autonomous Racing}

\author{Anonymous Authors,~\IEEEmembership{Member,~IEEE}}

\affil{This work was developed in the context of the Abu Dhabi Autonomous Racing
League (A2RL) on the Yas Marina Circuit using the Dallara EAV24 platform.}

\keywords{Autonomous racing, model predictive contouring control, second-order cone
programming, reinforcement learning, Frenet frame, friction circle,
game-theoretic motion planning.}

\begin{abstract}
Wheel-to-wheel autonomous racing couples strategic interaction, real-time
trajectory optimization, and friction-limit control.  Solving these decisions
in a single monolithic game or nonlinear program is computationally fragile at
Formula-class speeds, whereas end-to-end neural control is difficult to audit
on a safety-critical vehicle.  This paper presents a hierarchical architecture
that restricts learning to tactical corridor synthesis and retains
optimization-based planning and control downstream.  A map-conditioned
Truncated Quantile Critics policy and a hysteretic finite-state machine select
an overtake side, velocity cap, and time-varying Frenet corridor.  A sparse
second-order cone program computes a curvature-regularized lateral path within
that corridor, a forward--backward sweep imposes a super-elliptic GG
friction envelope, and a nonlinear model predictive contouring controller
tracks the resulting reference with a terminal progress reward and a soft
rear-axle friction constraint.  Closed-loop simulations on the Dallara EAV24
at Yas Marina demonstrate two successful fast-closure overtakes, with pass
times of $1.13\,\mathrm{s}$ and $8.63\,\mathrm{s}$ after entering
\textsc{Overtake}, peak speed up to $73.10\,\mathrm{m/s}$, and lateral RMS
tracking errors of $0.02$ and $0.05\,\mathrm{m}$.  The results support the
central premise that
learning should shape the feasible set, while model-based layers enforce
vehicle dynamics and handling-limit execution.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =====================================================================
\IEEEPARstart{A}{utonomous} racing at the vehicle handling limit is among
the most demanding closed-loop control problems in robotics.  A Formula-class
car must decide \emph{whether} to overtake, \emph{which side} to attempt,
\emph{where} to place the resulting trajectory within the track boundaries,
and \emph{how} to execute it at the friction limit---all while an adversarial
opponent simultaneously optimizes its own plan.  These four decisions span
discrete game theory, continuous trajectory optimization, and high-bandwidth
nonlinear control, operating across timescales that differ by three orders of
magnitude.  The Abu Dhabi Autonomous Racing League (A2RL), with its
$5.28\,\mathrm{km}$ Yas Marina Formula~1 circuit and the $700\,\mathrm{kg}$
Dallara EAV24 platform, provides a uniquely demanding testbed for this
hierarchical challenge~\cite{betz2023autonomous,wischnewski2022indy,jung2023iac}.

Classical autonomous driving architectures decompose the problem into
perception, behavior planning, motion planning, and
control~\cite{paden2016survey,gonzalez2016review}.  The behavior planner
selects a discrete maneuver---follow, change lane, yield---which the motion
planner translates into a continuous trajectory~\cite{werling2010frenet,ziegler2014baidu}.
In racing, however, the motion planner has traditionally been cast as a
single-vehicle, offline lap-time optimization
problem~\cite{christ2021,heilmeier2020,kapania2016,lipp2014,verschueren2014},
producing a fixed raceline tracked by an MPC or MPCC
controller~\cite{liniger2015,liniger2019rss,kabzan2019gpmpc,rosolia2020lmpc,brunner2017}.
Full-stack racing platforms developed for the Indy Autonomous Challenge and
A2RL document this paradigm in
detail~\cite{kabzan2020amzracing,betz2023autonomous,jung2023iac,raji2024kth}.
The fundamental limitation is that a pre-computed raceline cannot adapt to
an opponent who is simultaneously optimizing their own trajectory: an
adversary that defends the apex creates a geometric deadlock that a
single-vehicle planner cannot resolve without a higher-level instruction
about which side to attempt the pass and when to commit.

Recognising that wheel-to-wheel racing is inherently a non-cooperative
dynamic game, several groups have proposed game-theoretic formulations of the
tactical layer.  Wang \emph{et al.}~\cite{wang2021iterative} cast
head-to-head racing as an iterative best-response game with local Nash
convergence guarantees.  Iterative linear-quadratic game
solvers~\cite{fridovich2020ilqgames,laine2023} compute approximate
generalized feedback Nash equilibria at near-real-time rates.  Real-time
game-theoretic planners~\cite{spica2020} and viability-theoretic
approaches~\cite{liniger2020stackelberg,liniger2019rss} provide formal safety
certificates, while sampling-based MPPI~\cite{williams2018best} and Bayesian
intent inference~\cite{jia2023bayesianrace,sunberg2017pomdp} address
opponent-model uncertainty.  Despite this progress, pure game-theoretic
methods face four practical obstacles in Formula-class deployment:
(i)~exact Nash equilibria of continuous-time differential games are
intractable, and approximate solvers~\cite{fridovich2020ilqgames,laine2023}
may not converge within a single planning cycle at circuit speeds;
(ii)~the Nash solution is fragile to misspecification of the opponent's cost
function; (iii)~the discrete decision to switch from shadowing to overtaking
is not naturally captured by smooth equilibrium computations; and
(iv)~hand-tuned weights and horizon lengths must be recalibrated for each
track and pace configuration~\cite{betz2022software,betz2022survey}.

In parallel, data-driven methods have emerged as an alternative.  Sony's
GT~Sophy~\cite{wurman2022gtsophy} and subsequent RL-for-racing
work~\cite{fuchs2021superhuman,song2021autonomous,kaufmann2023champion}
demonstrated that end-to-end neural policies can achieve superhuman
performance in simulation.  Model-based RL~\cite{brunnbauer2022learning,schwarting2021},
safe RL~\cite{evans2023safelearning,brunke2022safe,he2023ralrace},
and learning-from-demonstration~\cite{remonda2024,rosolia2018sample}
have further expanded the data-driven toolkit.  Lin \emph{et
al.}~\cite{lin2024racellm} compared optimal control and RL at the handling
limit, finding that RL excels in multi-agent settings but underperforms in
single-vehicle lap time.  However, deploying end-to-end neural controllers
on a physical Formula car raises four serious concerns: (i)~the EAV24's
nonlinear tire model, aerodynamic downforce map, and differential torque
vectoring are not faithfully reproduced in open simulators; (ii)~a neural
network that outputs steering torque cannot be audited or certified by race
engineers; (iii)~actor-critic policies produce high-frequency command jitter
that degrades tire wear; and (iv)~without an explicit spatial
representation, the network must re-discover track geometry from reward
signals alone~\cite{remonda2024,kabzan2019gpmpc}.

We resolve these tensions through architectural separation: the RL policy is
restricted to the tactical layer, leaving model-based solvers for planning
and control.  A Truncated Quantile Critics (TQC) policy outputs a
low-dimensional action vector that reshapes the feasible lateral corridor
$\mathcal{C}_k=[L_{\min,k},L_{\max,k}]$ and the FSM hysteresis thresholds,
without ever modifying the planner cost or the controller dynamics.
Critically, the TQC observation vector embeds a \emph{fixed-track prior}: a
frozen, map-level encoding of raceline curvature, track banking, and
friction-limit speed at each arc-length station.  This spatial prior supplies
the policy with geometric context, so the learned tactical decision is
conditioned on upcoming curvature and available track width rather than on
reward history alone.  A heuristic FSM translates the continuous TQC output
into a discrete mode sequence and a corridor-carving decision, delivering a
constraint triple $(\mathcal{C}_k, v_{\mathrm{cap}}, \texttt{mode})$ to the
planner.

Given the corridor, the planning layer solves a sparse second-order cone
program (SOCP) for a curvature-smooth lateral path, followed by a
forward--backward speed sweep on a super-elliptic GG diagram with
empirically identified exponent $p=0.75$.  This two-stage decomposition is
substantially faster than monolithic nonlinear trajectory
optimization~\cite{verschueren2014} while naturally capturing the coupled
longitudinal--lateral grip limits.  The resulting reference is tracked by a
nonlinear Model Predictive Contouring Controller (MPCC) that augments an
$8$-state dynamic bicycle model with a path-progress state; a terminal
progress reward automatically generates corner entry-braking and
exit-acceleration without per-corner reference shaping, and a soft rear-axle
friction-cone constraint prevents tire saturation without rendering the
nonlinear program infeasible.  All three layers share a common Frenet
representation.

The contributions of this paper are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Map-conditioned tactical corridor synthesis.}
A TQC policy receives a frozen preview of curvature, track width, and
friction-limited speed and outputs only interpretable tactical quantities:
side preference, safety margin, velocity cap, and corridor bounds.  A
hysteretic FSM converts this output into a time-varying feasible set
$\mathcal{C}_k$, preventing the learned policy from directly commanding
actuators or altering downstream costs.

\item \textbf{Convex local path planning with GG-constrained speed profiling.}
A sparse SOCP over Frenet state and curvature slack produces a
curvature-regularized path inside the tactical corridor.  A
forward--backward sweep on a super-elliptic GG envelope then constructs the
corresponding grip-limited speed profile, separating geometric feasibility
from speed feasibility while preserving their coupling through curvature.

\item \textbf{Closed-loop validation of the hierarchy in head-to-head racing.}
An $8$-state dynamic-bicycle MPCC with a terminal progress reward and a soft
rear-axle friction constraint executes the planned references.  Two
closed-loop Yas Marina fast-closure overtake simulations quantify pass time,
tactical gain, peak speed, and tracking error, showing that the same planner
and controller can execute distinct tactical corridors without case-specific
retuning.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Vehicle and Frenet Modeling}\label{sec:model}
% =====================================================================
We adopt the convention that the controller sees a body-frame dynamic
single-track model (used inside MPCC), while the planner sees a Frenet
kinematic model derived from the same physical vehicle.  Both reduce to a
common parameterization in the limit of zero side-slip.

\subsection{Body-Frame Dynamic Single-Track Model}
\label{subsec:bicycle}
Let $\bm{x}\in\R^{8}$ and $\bm{u}\in\R^{2}$ denote the MPCC state and
input,
\begin{equation}
\bm{x}=\bigl[\,p_x,\;p_y,\;\psi,\;v_x,\;v_y,\;r,\;\delta,\;s\,\bigr]^{\!\top},\qquad
\bm{u}=\bigl[\,a,\;\dot{\delta}\,\bigr]^{\!\top},
\label{eq:state}
\end{equation}
where $(p_x,p_y)$ is the position in the inertial frame attached to the
\emph{prediction window origin}, $\psi$ is the yaw angle, $(v_x,v_y)$ are
the body-frame longitudinal and lateral velocities, $r=\dot{\psi}$ is the
yaw rate, $\delta$ is the front steering angle, $s$ is an integrated
longitudinal-progress state used by the contouring cost, $a$ is the
commanded longitudinal acceleration and $\dot{\delta}$ is the steering
rate. The progress state is integrated as $\dot{s}=v_x$, so $s$ acts as
the path parameter inside the contouring formulation of
Sec.~\ref{sec:controller}.

The kinematic relations are
\begin{equation}
\dot{p}_x=v_x\cos\psi-v_y\sin\psi,\quad
\dot{p}_y=v_x\sin\psi+v_y\cos\psi,\quad
\dot{\psi}=r.
\label{eq:kin}
\end{equation}
Front and rear slip angles are obtained with the standard small-angle
single-track expressions, regularized at low speed by an offset
$\varepsilon_v=0.5\,\mathrm{m/s}$,
\begin{align}
\alpha_f &= \arctan\!\left(\frac{v_y+l_f r}{v_x+\varepsilon_v}\right)-\delta,
\label{eq:alphaf}\\
\alpha_r &= \arctan\!\left(\frac{v_y-l_r r}{v_x+\varepsilon_v}\right).
\label{eq:alphar}
\end{align}
Linear cornering stiffness yields the lateral tire forces
\begin{equation}
F_{yf}=-C_f\,\alpha_f,\qquad F_{yr}=-C_r\,\alpha_r.
\label{eq:tire}
\end{equation}
Substituting into Newton--Euler gives the dynamic equations
\begin{align}
\dot{v}_x &= a + r\,v_y - \tfrac{1}{m}F_{yf}\sin\delta,
\label{eq:vxdot}\\
\dot{v}_y &= \tfrac{1}{m}\bigl(F_{yf}\cos\delta+F_{yr}\bigr) - r\,v_x,
\label{eq:vydot}\\
\dot{r}   &= \tfrac{1}{I_z}\bigl(l_f F_{yf}\cos\delta - l_r F_{yr}\bigr),
\label{eq:rdot}\\
\dot{\delta}&=\dot{\delta}.
\label{eq:deltadot}
\end{align}
The full continuous dynamics are written compactly as
\begin{equation}
\dot{\bm{x}}=f_c(\bm{x},\bm{u}),
\label{eq:fc}
\end{equation}
and discretized with explicit Runge--Kutta of order four with sample
$T_s=0.05\,\mathrm{s}$,
\begin{equation}
\bm{x}_{k+1}=f_d(\bm{x}_k,\bm{u}_k)\;\triangleq\;\mathrm{RK4}\!\bigl(f_c,\bm{x}_k,\bm{u}_k,T_s\bigr).
\label{eq:fd}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Frenet Kinematic Model and GG Envelope}
\label{subsec:frenet}
The reference centerline (or raceline) is a smooth curve
$\bm{r}(s)\in\R^{2}$ parameterized by arc-length $s\in[0,L_{\rm trk})$,
with curvature $\kappa(s)$ and tangent angle $\theta(s)$ such that
$\theta'(s)=\kappa(s)$.  Any point in a tube around $\bm{r}$ admits the
Frenet decomposition
\begin{equation}
\begin{bmatrix} X\\Y\end{bmatrix}
=\bm{r}(s)+l\,\bm{n}(s),\qquad
\bm{n}(s)=\bigl[-\sin\theta(s),\;\cos\theta(s)\bigr]^{\!\top},
\label{eq:frenet}
\end{equation}
where $l$ is the signed lateral offset and $\alpha\triangleq\psi-\theta(s)$
the heading deviation.  Differentiating~(\ref{eq:frenet}) and projecting
onto the tangent/normal frame yields, for a nonholonomic body with body
speed $v$,
\begin{align}
\dot{s} &= \frac{v\cos\alpha}{1-l\,\kappa(s)},
\label{eq:sdot_frenet}\\
\dot{l} &= v\sin\alpha,
\label{eq:ldot_frenet}\\
\dot{\alpha} &= \dot{\psi}-\kappa(s)\,\dot{s}.
\label{eq:adot_frenet}
\end{align}

\paragraph*{Spatial reformulation}
Replacing time by arc-length and using $\frac{d}{ds}=\frac{1}{\dot{s}}\frac{d}{dt}$,
\begin{equation}
l'(s)=\frac{(1-l\kappa)\tan\alpha}{1},\qquad
\alpha'(s)=\frac{(1-l\kappa)\,\dot{\psi}}{v\cos\alpha}-\kappa(s).
\label{eq:spatial}
\end{equation}
Linearizing~(\ref{eq:spatial}) around a nominal $(l_i,\alpha_i,\kappa_i)$
provides the planner-side dynamics
\begin{equation}
\frac{d}{ds}\!\begin{bmatrix} l\\ \alpha\end{bmatrix}
\;\approx\;
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} -\kappa_i\tan\alpha_i & \frac{1-\kappa_i l_i}{\cos^{2}\alpha_i}\\
0 & 0\end{bmatrix}}_{\bm{A}_i}
\!\begin{bmatrix} l\\ \alpha\end{bmatrix}
+
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0\\ \frac{1-\kappa_i l_i}{v_{\rm ref}^{2}\cos\alpha_i} & 0\end{bmatrix}}_{\bm{B}_i}
\!\begin{bmatrix} \zeta\\ \sigma\end{bmatrix}
+\bm{b}_i,
\label{eq:linAB}
\end{equation}
which is the building block of the SOCP discretization in
Sec.~\ref{sec:planner}.  The auxiliary variables $(\zeta,\sigma)$ are the
curvature slack and its second-order cone epigraph variable.

\paragraph*{GG envelope}
The combined-slip envelope of the EAV24 is well captured by the
super-elliptic relation
\begin{equation}
\left|\frac{a_y}{a_y^{\max}(v)}\right|^{p}+
\left|\frac{a_x}{a_x^{\max}(v)}\right|^{p}\le 1,
\label{eq:gg}
\end{equation}
where $a_y^{\max}(v)$ and $a_x^{\max}(v)$ are speed-dependent grip caps
obtained from a vehicle data table, and $p=0.75$ is identified empirically
from on-track lateral/longitudinal traces.  Solving~(\ref{eq:gg}) for the
available longitudinal acceleration gives
\begin{equation}
a_x^{\rm avail}(v,a_y)=a_x^{\max}(v)\!\left[\,1-\bigl(\tfrac{|a_y|}{a_y^{\max}(v)}\bigr)^{p}\,\right]^{1/p},
\label{eq:gg_axavail}
\end{equation}
which is the central nonlinearity of the FB speed planner.  Aerodynamic drag is modeled as a quadratic function of speed,
\begin{equation}
a_{\rm drag}(v)=\tfrac{1}{m}\bigl(c_{d,2}\,v^{2}+c_{d,1}\,v+c_{d,0}\bigr),
\label{eq:drag}
\end{equation}
whose coefficients $(c_{d,2},c_{d,1},c_{d,0})$ are identified from coastdown
data and listed in Table~\ref{tab:veh}.  Drag is added to the longitudinal
force balance with the sign convention of (\ref{eq:vxdot}).

\section{Tactical Layer: RL-Driven Corridor Synthesis}\label{sec:tactical}
% =====================================================================
The tactical layer transforms the multi-agent racing scene into a
spatio-temporally smooth feasible corridor for the planner.  It is the only
layer that observes opponents, and it communicates with the optimizer through
constraints rather than through cost weights or low-level commands.  This
interface keeps the learned component interpretable: changing the policy
changes where the optimizer is allowed to drive, not how the optimizer values
tracking, smoothness, or vehicle dynamics.

\subsection{Hybrid TQC Policy, Track Prior and Finite-State Machine}
\label{subsec:tactical_rl}
\paragraph*{Observation}
At each tactical step the environment supplies a compact dynamic observation
$\bm{o}_t\in\R^{27}$.  The ego-vehicle block comprises normalized speed,
heading error, lateral offset, and body-frame accelerations,
\begin{equation}
\bm{o}_t^{\rm ego}=\bigl[\bar{V},\;\chi,\;\bar{n},\;\bar{a}_x,\;\bar{a}_y,\;\bar{s}_e\bigr]^{\!\top},
\label{eq:obs_ego}
\end{equation}
where each component is scaled by its characteristic range (e.g.\
$\bar{V}=V/V_{\rm char}$, $\bar{n}=n/n_{\rm char}$); the specific scaling
constants are provided in Table~\ref{tab:obs_norm}.  The nearest opponent
in wrapped arc-length contributes a symmetrically normalized block,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\bm{o}_t^{\rm opp}=\bigl[&\bar{\Delta s},\;\bar{\Delta n},\;\bar{\Delta V_s},\;\bar{s}_o,\;
\bar{n}_o,\;\bar{g},\;\bar{\tau}_{\rm tc},\;\bar{V}_o\bigr]^{\!\top},
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:obs_opp}
\end{equation}
with the wrapped relative progress
\begin{equation}
\Delta s=\bigl((s_o-s_e+L_{\rm trk}/2)\bmod L_{\rm trk}\bigr)-L_{\rm trk}/2,
\quad \Delta n=n_o-n_e,
\label{eq:rel}
\end{equation}
$\Delta V_s=V_o-V_e$, the Euclidean gap $g=\sqrt{\Delta s^{2}+\Delta n^{2}}$,
and the time-to-collision $\tau_{\rm tc}=\Delta s/\max(-\Delta V_s,\varepsilon_\tau)$
for $\Delta V_s<0$.  Three one-hot closing-speed buckets
$\bm{b}_t\in\{0,1\}^{3}$, the locked overtake-side indicator
$\sigma_t^{\rm lock}\in\{-1,0,+1\}$, and the FSM-mode one-hot encoding
$\bm{e}_{m_t}\in\{0,1\}^{|\mathcal{M}|}$ complete the observation vector
\begin{equation}
\bm{o}_t=\bigl[\bm{o}_t^{\rm ego},\bm{o}_t^{\rm opp},\bm{b}_t,\sigma_t^{\rm lock},\bm{e}_{m_t}\bigr]\in\R^{27}.
\label{eq:obs27}
\end{equation}

\paragraph*{Neural fixed-track prior}
The policy feature extractor owns a fixed map of the circuit inside the
neural network.  A non-trainable lookup table
$\mathcal{P}=\{\bm{\rho}_i\}_{i=0}^{M-1}$ is constructed offline from the
baseline raceline before training.  Querying this table at arc-length $s$
returns a compact encoding of the track geometry over the next $d_p$ metres,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\bm{\rho}(s)=\bigl[&\bar{\kappa}_0,\;
\bar{\kappa}_{0:d_1}^{\max},\;
\bar{\kappa}_{d_1:d_2}^{\max},\;
\bar{\kappa}_{d_2:d_3}^{\max},\;
\bar{\kappa}_{d_3:d_p}^{\max},\;
\bar{\kappa}_{\rm mean},\;
\bar{\kappa}_{\rm std},\\
&\bar{W}_{\min}^{\rm L},\;
\bar{W}_{\min}^{\rm R},\;
\bar{W}_{0}^{\rm L},\;
\bar{W}_{0}^{\rm R},\;
\bar{\Delta W}_{\min}\bigr]^{\!\top},
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:track_prior}
\end{equation}
where the first five entries are the local curvature and the peak absolute
curvatures in successive $d_{\rm win}$-metre windows; the remaining entries
encode current and bottleneck left/right track half-widths, each
normalized by the characteristic track width $W_{\rm char}$.  The actor
and critics therefore receive the map-conditioned input
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t=\bigl[\bm{o}_t,\bm{\rho}(s_e),\bm{\rho}(s_o)\bigr]\in\R^{d_o+2d_\rho},
\label{eq:track_aug_obs}
\end{equation}
processed by a feedforward feature network.  Critically, the track map is
embedded in the neural network as a fixed spatial prior indexed by ego and
opponent arc-length; it is not a hand-coded planner cost term.

\paragraph*{Action}
The TQC actor outputs a bounded vector $\bm{u}_t\in[-1,1]^{d_a}$, which is
affine-mapped elementwise to the physical action
\begin{equation}
\bm{a}_t=\underline{\bm{a}}+\tfrac{1}{2}(\bm{u}_t+\bm{1})\odot(\overline{\bm{a}}-\underline{\bm{a}}),
\label{eq:tanh_map}
\end{equation}
where the action vector
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\bm{a}_t=\bigl[&L_t^{\rm cap},\,R_t^{\rm cap},\,d_{\rm safe},\,V_{\rm cap},\,
g_{\rm chase},\,g_{\rm ot},\\
&g_{\rm abort},\,b_{\rm lat},\,w_{\rm safe},\,q_{\rm side}\bigr]^{\!\top}
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:action_vec}
\end{equation}
comprises the left/right corridor caps $(L_t^{\rm cap},R_t^{\rm cap})$, the
safety margin $d_{\rm safe}$, the velocity cap $V_{\rm cap}$, the FSM
hysteresis thresholds $(g_{\rm chase},g_{\rm ot},g_{\rm abort})$, a lateral
bias $b_{\rm lat}$, a corridor-width bonus $w_{\rm safe}$, and a side-selection
score $q_{\rm side}\in[-1,1]$ whose sign determines the overtake direction
($q_{\rm side}\ge0$: left; $q_{\rm side}<0$: right).  The side decision
is locked for the duration of one duel.  The elementwise bounds
$\underline{\bm{a}},\overline{\bm{a}}$ are provided in
Table~\ref{tab:action_bounds}.  After affine mapping, consistency clips
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
g_{\rm chase}\ge g_{\rm ot}-\varepsilon_{co},\quad
g_{\rm ot}\ge g_{\rm chase}-\varepsilon_{oc},\\
g_{\rm abort}\ge g_{\rm chase}+\varepsilon_{ca},\quad
L_t^{\rm cap}+R_t^{\rm cap}\ge\varepsilon_{\rm corr}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:safety_clip}
\end{equation}
are enforced with small positive offsets $(\varepsilon_{co},\varepsilon_{oc},\varepsilon_{ca},\varepsilon_{\rm corr})$
to maintain a non-empty corridor and a well-ordered FSM throughout training.

\paragraph*{Finite-state machine}
The discrete tactical mode $m_t\in\mathcal{M}=\{$\textsc{Raceline,
Shadow, Overtake, Hold, Defend}$\}$ is selected by a hysteretic FSM.
Let $g$ denote the longitudinal gap to the leading opponent and
$g_{\rm rear}$ the gap to the closest trailing opponent.  The FSM
transition logic is
\begin{equation}
m_{t+1}=
\begin{cases}
\textsc{Overtake} & g\le g_{\rm ot}\,\wedge\,\textit{ready}\,\wedge\,\bar c\\[2pt]
\textsc{Shadow}   & g_{\rm ot}<g\le g_{\rm chase}\\[2pt]
\textsc{Hold}     & m_t\!=\!\textsc{Overtake}\wedge\textit{abort}\\[2pt]
\textsc{Defend}   & g>g_{\rm def}^{\rm front}\wedge 0<g_{\rm rear}<g_{\rm def}^{\rm rear}\wedge\bar c\\[2pt]
\textsc{Raceline} & g>g_{\rm chase},
\end{cases}
\label{eq:fsm}
\end{equation}
where $\bar c$ indicates that the post-overtake immunity window has
expired.  The abort criterion is
$(g>g_{\rm abort})\vee(\Delta n_{\min}<\Delta n_{\rm safe}\wedge g<g_{\rm interlock})$,
which guards against late-corner divergence and lateral interlock with the
opponent.  The thresholds $(g_{\rm chase},g_{\rm ot},g_{\rm abort})$ are
the RL-modulated hysteresis parameters from $\bm{a}_t$, while
$(g_{\rm def}^{\rm front},g_{\rm def}^{\rm rear},\Delta n_{\rm safe},g_{\rm interlock})$
are fixed constants whose values are reported in
Table~\ref{tab:fsm_constants}.

\begin{equation}

\end{equation}

approach incentive
$\phi_{\rm app}=\mathbb{1}[g<g_{\rm app}\wedge\dot g<0]\,/(1+g)$ that

$\phi_{\rm gs}=(\Delta L^{\rm cap}_t)^{2}+(\Delta R^{\rm cap}_t)^{2}+(\Delta b_{\rm lat,t})^{2}$

The policy is optimized with Truncated Quantile Critics.
Each critic represents a distributional action value by $N_q$ quantile atoms
$\{z_{\phi_i}^{j}(\widetilde{\bm{o}},\bm{u})\}_{j=1}^{N_q}$; the truncated
target distribution discards the largest atoms before Bellman backup,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{T}_{\rm trunc}=\operatorname{sort}\Bigl(\{z_{\bar\phi_i}^{j}(\widetilde{\bm{o}}',\bm{u}')-\alpha\log\pi_\theta(\bm{u}'|\widetilde{\bm{o}}')\}_{i,j}\Bigr)_{1:K},
\label{eq:tqc_trunc}
\end{equation}
where $K<N_q$ is the number of retained quantile atoms.  The critic
minimizes the quantile Huber loss
\begin{equation}
J_Q(\phi)=\sum_{i,j}\E_{\mathcal{D},\,y\in r+\gamma\mathcal{T}_{\rm trunc}}
\left[\rho^{\kappa}_{\hat\tau_j}\!\left(y-z_{\phi_i}^{j}(\widetilde{\bm{o}},\bm{u})\right)\right],
\label{eq:tqc_critic}
\end{equation}
while the actor maximizes the mean truncated value with an entropy
regularizer,
\begin{equation}
J_\pi(\theta)=\E_{\widetilde{\bm{o}}\sim\mathcal{D},\,\bm{u}\sim\pi_\theta}\!\left[\alpha\log\pi_\theta(\bm{u}|\widetilde{\bm{o}})-\frac{1}{K}\sum_{z\in\mathcal{T}_{\rm trunc}(\widetilde{\bm{o}},\bm{u})}z\right].
\label{eq:tqc_actor}
\end{equation}
The temperature $\alpha$ is adjusted automatically to maintain a target
entropy.  Training hyperparameters are summarized in
Table~\ref{tab:tqc_hyperparams}.

\subsection{Heading-Aware Corridor Carving}
\label{subsec:carver}
The corridor carver is a deterministic function
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{F}_{\rm carve}:\;&
(\bm{o}_t,\bm{a}_t,m_t,\{\bm{o}_t^{(j)}\}_{j=1}^{N_{\rm opp}})\\
&\mapsto\{(s_k,L_{\min,k},L_{\max,k})\}_{k=0}^{N_{\rm sta}-1}
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:carver_map}
\end{equation}
that converts opponent states and the FSM mode into a corridor of
$N_{\rm sta}$ stations with uniform spacing $\Delta s_{\rm sta}$ over the
planning horizon $d_p$.  All numerical constants referenced below are
collected in Table~\ref{tab:corridor_constants}.

\paragraph*{Heading-aware projection}
For opponent $j$ with Frenet heading error $\chi^{(j)}$ and rectangular
footprint of length $L_v$ and width $W_v$, the half-extents in the Frenet
$(s,n)$ axes are
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\rho_n^{(j)}&=\tfrac12\bigl(L_v|\sin\chi^{(j)}|+W_v|\cos\chi^{(j)}|\bigr),\\
\rho_s^{(j)}&=\tfrac12\bigl(L_v|\cos\chi^{(j)}|+W_v|\sin\chi^{(j)}|\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:proj}
\end{equation}
The lateral exclusion radius around opponent $j$ is then
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\rm exc}^{(j)}=\rho_n^{(j)}+\tfrac{W_v}{2}+d_{\rm safe},
\label{eq:rho_exc}
\end{equation}
where $d_{\rm safe}$ is the RL-modulated safety margin from $\bm{a}_t$.

\paragraph*{Spatial fading and startup protection}
Opponent influence is attenuated by a smooth fading kernel over distance,
\begin{equation}
\phi_{\rm fade}(\Delta s)=
\begin{cases}
\cos^{p_{\rm fade}}\!\Bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{2}\tfrac{|\Delta s|}{S_{\rm fade}}\Bigr),
& |\Delta s|<S_{\rm fade},\\[4pt]
0, & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\label{eq:fade}
\end{equation}
with characteristic range $S_{\rm fade}$.  A linear startup ramp
$\phi_{\rm sr}(k)$ prevents corridor collapse in the first few stations
immediately ahead of the ego, where the planner has insufficient spatial
authority to react.

\paragraph*{Mode-conditioned corridor update}
Let $L_{\max,k}^{(0)},L_{\min,k}^{(0)}$ be the geometric track-edge
half-widths at station $k$.  In \textsc{Overtake} mode with committed pass
side $\sigma\in\{+1,-1\}$, the carver tightens the opponent-side bound,
\begin{equation}
L_{\sigma,k}\leftarrow\sigma\bigl(\sigma\, n_{o,k}+\rho_{\rm exc}^{(j)}\,\phi_{\rm fade}\,\phi_{\rm sr}\,\phi_{\rm rmp}\bigr),
\label{eq:overtake_bound}
\end{equation}
where $n_{o,k}$ is the predicted opponent lateral position at $s_k$ and
$\phi_{\rm rmp}(k)$ is a distance-dependent blending ramp.  In
\textsc{Shadow} mode the corridor is constructed as an asymmetric funnel
that widens from the ego toward the pass side; the funnel width at
convergence and the near-ego bonus are controlled by the parameters
$(W_{\rm fun},W_{\rm near},\lambda_{\rm side})$, with $\lambda_{\rm side}$
governing the pass-side allocation fraction.  Across all modes a minimum
corridor width $L_{\min}^{\rm corr}$ is enforced, and the resulting bound
profile is temporally smoothed by an exponential moving average with
coefficient $\beta\in(0,1)$,
\begin{equation}
\bigl[L_{\min,k},L_{\max,k}\bigr]_t\;\leftarrow\;\beta\,\bigl[\,\cdot\,\bigr]_t+(1-\beta)\bigl[\,\cdot\,\bigr]_{t-1}.
\label{eq:ema}
\end{equation}

\paragraph*{RL bias blending}
The RL action also emits a terminal lateral bias $b_{\rm lat}$ and global
caps $(L_t^{\rm cap},R_t^{\rm cap})$.  These are blended into the carver
output through a quadratic weight $w_k$ that vanishes at the ego
($k=0$) and reaches unity at a short look-ahead index $k_{\rm blend}$,
\begin{equation}
w_k = (k/k_{\rm blend})^{2}\,\mathbb{1}[k<k_{\rm blend}]+\mathbb{1}[k\ge k_{\rm blend}],
\label{eq:rl_blend_w}
\end{equation}
\begin{align}
L_{\max,k} &\leftarrow (1-w_k)L_{\max,k}+w_k\min\bigl(L_{\max,k},\,b_{\rm lat}+L_t^{\rm cap}\bigr),
\label{eq:rl_blend_max}\\
L_{\min,k} &\leftarrow (1-w_k)L_{\min,k}+w_k\max\bigl(L_{\min,k},\,b_{\rm lat}-R_t^{\rm cap}\bigr).
\label{eq:rl_blend_min}
\end{align}
This blending is the mechanism through which the RL action directly shapes
the planner corridor; the safety margin and side decision enter upstream
through the carver, and all three are ultimately realized as
$\mathcal{C}_k$.

\section{Local Planning Layer}\label{sec:planner}
% =====================================================================
Given the corridor $\mathcal{C}=\{(s_k,L_{\min,k},L_{\max,k})\}$ from the
tactical layer, the planner produces a curvature-smooth lateral profile
$l(s)$ inside $\mathcal{C}$ via a SOCP, and a maximum-grip speed profile
$v(s)$ via a forward--backward sweep on the GG super-ellipse.  Both
solvers are exact and convex on their respective subproblems; convexity
is preserved by sequential linearization of the only nonlinearity in
(\ref{eq:linAB}).

\subsection{SOCP Lateral Path Planning}
\label{subsec:socp}
\paragraph*{Decision variable}
At each of $N$ stations $k=0,\dots,N-1$ along the look-ahead horizon
$d_p$, with uniform spacing $\Delta s_k=d_p/(N-1)$, the planner
instantiates four scalars,
\begin{equation}
\bm{y}_k=\bigl[\,l_k,\;\alpha_k,\;\zeta_k,\;\sigma_k\,\bigr]^{\!\top}\in\R^{4},
\label{eq:yk}
\end{equation}
namely lateral offset $l_k$, heading deviation $\alpha_k$, curvature
slack $\zeta_k$, and the second-order cone witness $\sigma_k$.  The
stacked decision vector is $\bm{y}=[\bm{y}_0^{\!\top},\dots,\bm{y}_{N-1}^{\!\top}]^{\!\top}\in\R^{4N}$.
For numerical conditioning, all quantities are non-dimensionalized by a
characteristic length $r_{\rm sc}$ and the derived speed
$v_{\rm sc}=\sqrt{r_{\rm sc}\,g}$.

\paragraph*{Cost}
The cost penalizes only the cone witness,
\begin{equation}
J_{\rm SOCP}(\bm{y})=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}w_\sigma\,\sigma_k,
\label{eq:Jsoc}
\end{equation}
which, together with the cone constraint defined below, implements an
$L_2$ penalty on the curvature slack.  Path smoothness is induced
indirectly by the dynamic equality: $l_{k+1}-l_k$ is slaved to
$\alpha_k\Delta s_k$, which is in turn coupled to $\sigma_k$ through
(\ref{eq:linAB}); minimizing $\sigma_k$ therefore minimizes a quadratic
surrogate of $l''(s)$.

\paragraph*{Equality constraints}
Initial and (mode-dependent) terminal conditions pin the boundary,
\begin{equation}
l_0=l_{\rm ego},\;\;\alpha_0=\alpha_{\rm ego},\qquad
l_{N-1}=l_f^\star,\;\;\alpha_{N-1}=\alpha_f^\star,
\label{eq:bc}
\end{equation}
where the terminal target is selected as
\begin{equation}
l_f^\star=
\begin{cases}
l_{\rm raceline}(s_{N-1}) & \!\!\text{race mode, in-corridor},\\
\tfrac12(L_{\max,N-1}+L_{\min,N-1}) & \!\!\text{tactical/out-corridor}.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:terminal_mode}
\end{equation}
The Frenet dynamics (\ref{eq:linAB}) are discretized by trapezoidal
integration; for $i=0,\dots,N-2$,
\begin{multline}
\Bigl(\bm{I}+\tfrac{\Delta s_i}{2}\bm{A}_{1,i}\Bigr)\bm{\xi}_i
+\tfrac{\Delta s_i}{2}\bm{B}_{1,i}\bm{\eta}_i\\
-\Bigl(\bm{I}-\tfrac{\Delta s_i}{2}\bm{A}_{2,i}\Bigr)\bm{\xi}_{i+1}
+\tfrac{\Delta s_i}{2}\bm{B}_{2,i}\bm{\eta}_{i+1}\\
=-\tfrac{\Delta s_i}{2}\bigl(\bm{b}_{1,i}+\bm{b}_{2,i}\bigr),
\label{eq:trap}
\end{multline}
with $\bm{\xi}_i=[l_i,\alpha_i]^{\!\top}$, $\bm{\eta}_i=[\zeta_i,\sigma_i]^{\!\top}$
and $(\bm{A}_{j,i},\bm{B}_{j,i},\bm{b}_{j,i})$ the linearization
(\ref{eq:linAB}) at endpoints $j\in\{1,2\}$ of segment $i$.

\paragraph*{Linear inequality constraints (corridor)}
For each station $k$, the corridor injected by the tactical layer is
mapped into two affine half-planes,
\begin{equation}
L_{\min,k}+d_{\rm veh}\le l_k\le L_{\max,k}-d_{\rm veh},
\label{eq:corr_ineq}
\end{equation}
where $d_{\rm veh}$ accounts for the half-vehicle width plus a static
safety margin.  In race mode without external corridor injection,
$L_{\min,k},L_{\max,k}$ default to the geometric track edges reduced by
the on-line obstacle clearance.

\paragraph*{Second-order cone constraint}
The curvature slack and its epigraph variable are bound through a single
3-dimensional cone per station,
\begin{equation}
\Bigl\lVert\bigl[\,2\zeta_k,\;\sigma_k-1\,\bigr]^{\!\top}\Bigr\rVert_{2}
\le \sigma_k+1,\quad k=0,\dots,N\!-\!1,
\label{eq:cone}
\end{equation}
which is equivalent to $\zeta_k^2\le\sigma_k$ for
$\sigma_k\ge0$.  Combined with the linear cost in (\ref{eq:Jsoc}), the
constraint implements a convex quadratic epigraph penalty on curvature slack
without introducing a dense Hessian in the conic program.

\paragraph*{Compiled SOCP}
Defining $\bm{c}\in\R^{4N}$ with $\bm{c}_{4k+3}=w_\sigma$ and zero
elsewhere, $\bm{G}\in\R^{(2N+3N)\times 4N}$ stacking the corridor and cone
rows, and $\bm{A}\in\R^{(2+2(N-1)+2)\times 4N}$ stacking
(\ref{eq:bc})--(\ref{eq:trap}), the planner solves
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\bm{y}^{\star}=\argmin_{\bm{y}\in\R^{4N}}\;& \bm{c}^{\!\top}\bm{y}\\
\text{s.t.}\;& \bm{A}\bm{y}=\bm{b},\\
& \bm{G}\bm{y}\preceq_{\mathcal{K}}\bm{h},
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:socp}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{K}=\R^{2N}_{+}\times\mathcal{Q}_{3}^{N}$ is the product
of the linear inequality cone and $N$ second-order cones of dimension
three.  Both $\bm{G}$ and $\bm{A}$ are assembled directly in compressed
column storage.

\paragraph*{Sequential linearization}
The matrices in (\ref{eq:linAB}) depend on the operating point
$(l_i,\alpha_i,\kappa_i)$.  We resolve this by sequential linearization:
at iteration $j$, (\ref{eq:socp}) is solved with $(l_i^{(j)},
\alpha_i^{(j)})$ as the linearization point; the new iterate
$(l_i^{(j+1)},\alpha_i^{(j+1)})$ is read from $\bm{y}^{\star}$;
convergence is declared when
\begin{equation}
\max_i|l_i^{(j+1)}-l_i^{(j)}|<\varepsilon_l,\qquad
\max_i|\alpha_i^{(j+1)}-\alpha_i^{(j)}|<\varepsilon_\alpha,
\label{eq:slin_conv}
\end{equation}
or the iteration cap $J_{\max}$ is reached.  In practice, two to three
iterations suffice once the iterate is warm-started from the previous
planning cycle's solution.

\subsection{Forward--Backward GG-Coupled Velocity Profiling}
\label{subsec:fb}
The path $l^\star(s)$ obtained from (\ref{eq:socp}) is converted into a
$2$D curve $\bm{p}(s)=\bm{r}(s)+l^\star(s)\bm{n}(s)$ whose curvature
$\kappa^\star(s)$ is recomputed analytically.  The FB sweep then assigns a
speed profile $v(s)$ that maximizes lap pace while respecting the
super-elliptic GG envelope (\ref{eq:gg}) and the speed-dependent grip
caps $a_y^{\max}(v)$, $a_x^{\max}(v)$.

\paragraph*{Curvature ceiling}
At each station the maximum stationary speed is bounded by lateral grip,
$v_{\rm ce}^{2}(s)=a_y^{\max}(v_{\rm ce})/|\kappa^\star(s)|$.  The implicit
dependence of $a_y^{\max}$ on $v$ is resolved by fixed-point iteration,
\begin{equation}
v_{\rm ce}^{(j+1)}=\sqrt{a_y^{\max}\!\bigl(v_{\rm ce}^{(j)}\bigr)/|\kappa^\star(s)|},
\label{eq:vlim_iter}
\end{equation}
with stopping tolerance $\varepsilon_v$, typically converging in fewer than
five iterations.  The result is the station-wise curvature-ceiling array
$V_{\rm ce}[k]$.

\paragraph*{Forward pass (acceleration)}
Initialized at $V_f[0]=\min(v_{\rm ego},V_{\rm ce}[0])$, the forward
sweep integrates from $k=0$ to $N-2$ as
\begin{align}
a_{y,k} &= \kappa_k^\star\,V_f[k]^{2},
\label{eq:ay_fwd}\\
a_{x,k}^{\rm avail} &= a_x^{\max}(V_f[k])\notag\\
&\quad\times
\bigl[1-(|a_{y,k}|/a_y^{\max}(V_f[k]))^{p}\bigr]^{1/p},
\label{eq:axavail_fwd}\\
a_{x,k}^{\rm net}  &= \min\!\bigl(a_{x,k}^{\rm avail},a_{x,k}^{\rm engine}\bigr)+a_{\rm drag}(V_f[k]),
\label{eq:axnet_fwd}\\
V_f[k+1] &= \min\!\Bigl(\!\sqrt{V_f[k]^{2}+2\Delta s_k\,a_{x,k}^{\rm net}},\,V_{\rm ce}[k+1]\Bigr),
\label{eq:vfwd}
\end{align}
where $a_{x,k}^{\rm engine}$ is the powertrain ceiling read from the
PTP-on/off lookup tables and $a_{\rm drag}(\cdot)$ is the drag model of
(\ref{eq:drag}).  This sweep encodes the maximum-grip acceleration policy
under combined slip.

\paragraph*{Backward pass (braking)}
Terminated at $V_b[N-1]=\min(v_f,V_{\rm ce}[N-1])$, the backward sweep
runs from $k=N-2$ down to $0$,
\begin{align}
\tilde a_{y,k+1} &= \kappa_{k+1}^\star\,V_b[k+1]^{2},
\label{eq:ay_bwd}\\
\tilde a_{x,k+1}^{\rm brk} &= a_x^{\max}(V_b[k+1])\notag\\
&\quad\times
\bigl[1-(|\tilde a_{y,k+1}|/a_y^{\max}(V_b[k+1]))^{p}\bigr]^{1/p},
\label{eq:ax_bwd_brk}\\
\tilde a_{x,k+1}^{\rm tot} &= \tilde a_{x,k+1}^{\rm brk}-a_{\rm drag}(V_b[k+1]),
\label{eq:ax_bwd_tot}\\
V_b[k] &= \min\!\Bigl(\!\sqrt{V_b[k+1]^{2}+2\Delta s_k\,\tilde a_{x,k+1}^{\rm tot}},\,V_{\rm ce}[k]\Bigr).
\label{eq:vbwd}
\end{align}
Drag enters with opposite sign to (\ref{eq:axnet_fwd}) because it
\emph{assists} braking; this sign discipline is essential to match
on-track coastdown traces above $50\,\mathrm{m/s}$.

\paragraph*{Profile fusion and acceleration extraction}
The final speed profile is the pointwise minimum,
\begin{equation}
V[k]=\min\!\bigl\{V_f[k],\;V_b[k],\;V_{\rm ce}[k]\bigr\},
\label{eq:vmerge}
\end{equation}
clipped to $[v_{\min},v_{\max}]$ for numerical stability.  Tangential and
normal accelerations follow from energy conservation,
\begin{equation}
a_{T,k}=\frac{V[k+1]^{2}-V[k]^{2}}{2\Delta s_k},\qquad
a_{N,k}=\kappa_k^\star V[k]^{2}.
\label{eq:atan}
\end{equation}
A runtime aggressiveness factor $\eta\in(0,1]$ and an upstream velocity
cap $v_{\rm rc}$ are applied as the final scaling and clipping,
\begin{equation}
V_{\rm out}[k]=\min\!\bigl(\eta\,V[k],\;v_{\rm rc}\bigr).
\label{eq:vout}
\end{equation}
The output trajectory $\{(x_k,y_k,\psi_k,\kappa_k,V_{\rm out}[k],t_k)\}$,
with time stamps $t_{k+1}=t_k+\Delta s_k/V_{\rm out}[k]$, is resampled at the
controller cadence $\Delta t_{\rm out}$ to produce a reference of
$N_{\rm out}$ points.

\section{Control Layer: MPCC}\label{sec:controller}
% =====================================================================
The controller closes the loop on the planner output with a Model
Predictive Contouring Controller (MPCC) that exploits the dynamic
model~(\ref{eq:fc}) and explicitly trades tracking accuracy against
arc-length progress through a single positively-weighted terminal term.

\subsection{Stage Cost and Progress Maximization}
\label{subsec:cost}
At each control cycle of period $T_c=0.05\,\mathrm{s}$, the controller
solves a finite-horizon nonlinear program over $N_c=15$ stages, with a
horizon of $T_c\,N_c=0.75\,\mathrm{s}$.  The stage cost couples a
weighted lateral, yaw and speed tracking error with input regularization,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
L_k(\bm{x}_k,\bm{u}_k,\zeta_k)=&\;q_l e_{l,k}^{2}+q_\psi e_{\psi,k}^{2}+q_v e_{v,k}^{2}\\
&+R_a a_k^{2}+R_{\dot\delta}\dot\delta_k^{2}+w_\zeta\zeta_k^{2},
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:Lk}
\end{equation}
where the contouring/lag decomposition is
\begin{align}
e_{l,k} &= -\sin\psi_{r,k}(p_{x,k}-x_{r,k})\notag\\
        &\quad+\cos\psi_{r,k}(p_{y,k}-y_{r,k}),
\label{eq:el}\\
e_{\psi,k}&=\psi_k-\psi_{r,k},\qquad e_{v,k}=v_{x,k}-v_{r,k},
\label{eq:epsv}
\end{align}
with $(x_{r,k},y_{r,k},\psi_{r,k},v_{r,k})$ the reference quadruple from
the planner expressed in the body frame at $k=0$ to avoid the
ill-conditioning of large global positions.  The reference speed entering $e_{v,k}$ is clamped per stage,
\begin{equation}
v_{r,k}\leftarrow\mathrm{clamp}\bigl(v_{r,k},\;v_x-\Delta v_{\rm lo},\;v_x+\Delta v_{\rm hi}\bigr),
\label{eq:vref_clip}
\end{equation}
which prevents the velocity-tracking term from dominating during sharp
deceleration phases where the planner naturally lags behind the actual
vehicle speed.  The asymmetric bounds $(\Delta v_{\rm lo},\Delta v_{\rm hi})$
are chosen such that the controller is permitted to overshoot the reference
but penalized for undershooting.

The terminal cost is the unique asymmetric term,
\begin{equation}
\Phi(\bm{x}_{N_c})=-q_{\rm prog}\,s_{N_c},\qquad q_{\rm prog}=0.5,
\label{eq:phi}
\end{equation}
which rewards arc-length progress along the prediction window.  Because
$s$ is integrated as $\dot s=v_x$ in (\ref{eq:state}), maximizing
$s_{N_c}$ is equivalent to maximizing the path-tangent component of
average speed over the horizon, weighted toward the end of the horizon.
Combined with the friction-circle constraint introduced below, this term
is the mechanism by which the controller produces the canonical
entry-deceleration / apex-grip / exit-acceleration pattern without an
explicit corner-by-corner reference shaping.

once on the global raceline and held constant across all races.

\subsection{Friction-Circle Constrained NLP and Real-Time Solution}
\label{subsec:nlp}
\paragraph*{Constraints}
Equality constraints enforce the dynamics and the initial state,
\begin{equation}
\bm{x}_{k+1}=f_d(\bm{x}_k,\bm{u}_k),\;k=0,\dots,N_c-1;\quad \bm{x}_0=\bm{x}_{\rm init}.
\label{eq:dyn_eq}
\end{equation}
Box constraints on inputs and states are applied at every stage,
\begin{align}
&\underline a\le a_k\le\overline a,\;\;-\dot\delta_{\max}\le\dot\delta_k\le\dot\delta_{\max},
\label{eq:ubox}\\
&0\le v_{x,k}\le v_{x,\max},\;\;-\delta_{\max}\le\delta_k\le\delta_{\max}.
\label{eq:xbox}
\end{align}
Combined slip on the rear axle is enforced as a single soft second-order
cone per stage,
\begin{equation}
\bigl(m\,a_k\bigr)^{2}+\bigl(C_r\,\alpha_{r,k}\bigr)^{2}\le\bigl(\mu F_{z,r}+\zeta_k\bigr)^{2},\quad\zeta_k\ge0,
\label{eq:fric}
\end{equation}
with the rear normal load determined by static weight distribution,
\begin{equation}
F_{z,r}=\frac{m g\,l_f}{l_f+l_r}.
\label{eq:fzr}
\end{equation}
The slack $\zeta_k$ in~(\ref{eq:fric}) is the same variable that appears
in $L_k$ through $w_\zeta\zeta_k^{2}$; this places a quadratic price on
exceeding the friction envelope and is the primary mechanism by which the
controller stays inside~(\ref{eq:gg}) without rendering the NLP
infeasible during transient over-grip events.

\paragraph*{Compiled NLP}
Collecting the decision variables
$(\bm{X},\bm{U},\bm{\zeta})\in\R^{8(N_c+1)}\!\times\R^{2N_c}\!\times\R^{N_c}_+$,
the controller solves
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\min_{\bm{X},\bm{U},\bm{\zeta}}\;& \sum_{k=0}^{N_c-1}L_k(\bm{x}_k,\bm{u}_k,\zeta_k)+\Phi(\bm{x}_{N_c})\\
\text{s.t.}\;& \text{(\ref{eq:dyn_eq})--(\ref{eq:fric}) hold}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:nlp}
\end{equation}
The NLP is solved by a primal--dual interior-point method with adaptive
barrier strategy, warm-started from the previous cycle's solution shifted
by one stage.  An iteration limit and a wall-time budget are imposed to
guarantee deterministic returns within the control period.  The
steering-angle and acceleration commands are reconstructed from the
optimal solution as
\begin{equation}
\delta^\star_0=\delta_{\rm prev}+T_c\,\dot\delta^\star_0,\qquad a^\star_0=a^\star.
\label{eq:cmd}
\end{equation}

\paragraph*{Real-time monitoring and fallback}
The MPCC solution is accepted only when (i) the solver returns a success
status, (ii) the commanded acceleration and steering angle lie within
predefined safety bounds $|a^\star_0|\le a_{\max}$ and
$|\delta^\star_0|\le\delta_{\max}$, and (iii) the body speed is above the
minimum threshold for which the linearized tire model in (\ref{eq:tire})
remains valid.  If any check fails, the controller reverts to a legacy
lateral MPC plus longitudinal PI cascade for the current cycle and
reattempts MPCC on the next cycle.

% =====================================================================
\section{Real-Time Integration}\label{sec:integration}
% =====================================================================
The hierarchy is implemented as an acyclic cascade with explicit data
contracts between adjacent layers.  At tactical time $t$, the policy and FSM
publish a guidance tuple
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{G}_t&=\bigl(m_t,\;V_{{\rm cap},t},\;\mathcal{C}_t,\;\pi_t\bigr),\\
\mathcal{C}_t&=\{(s_k,L_{\min,k},L_{\max,k})\}_{k=0}^{N_{\rm sta}-1},
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:guidance_tuple}
\end{equation}
where $m_t$ selects the terminal condition in (\ref{eq:terminal_mode}),
$V_{{\rm cap},t}$ clips the FB speed profile through (\ref{eq:vout}),
$\mathcal{C}_t$ defines the lateral half-plane constraints
(\ref{eq:corr_ineq}), and $\pi_t$ stores race-control permissions such as
point-to-point passing availability.  The planner holds the last valid
guidance tuple if a tactical update is delayed; thus the feasible set evolves
piecewise smoothly rather than discontinuously.

The planner converts $\mathcal{G}_t$ into a time-parameterized reference
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}_t=\{(x_k,y_k,\psi_k,\kappa_k,V_k,\tau_k)\}_{k=0}^{N_{\rm out}-1},
\label{eq:reference_tuple}
\end{equation}
after the SOCP path solve and GG-constrained velocity sweep.  The MPCC
controller transforms $\mathcal{R}_t$ into the local body frame at each
control cycle, interpolates the reference on its prediction grid, and solves
(\ref{eq:nlp}).  This design leaves only two forms of authority flowing
downstream: the tactical layer may restrict the feasible set and speed cap,
and the planner may update the reference trajectory.  Neither layer can
overwrite controller constraints, tire limits, or actuator bounds.

The temporal hierarchy can therefore be summarized as
\begin{equation}
\underbrace{\text{strategy}}_{\text{tactical FSM tenure}}
\;\succ\;
\underbrace{\text{corridor}}_{\text{tactical/planner I/O}}
\;\succ\;
\underbrace{\text{MPCC}}_{\text{control loop}},
\label{eq:hierarchy}
\end{equation}
with each upper layer setting the admissible set of the layer below.  This is
the principal systems property used in the experimental analysis: tactical
success is judged by whether the learned corridor is executable by the
unchanged planner and controller, not by whether a learned controller happens
to survive a particular duel.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% =====================================================================
We have described a complete tactical--planning--control stack for
wheel-to-wheel autonomous racing on a Formula-class platform.  A unified
Frenet model derived from a dynamic single-track vehicle underpins all
three layers; a TQC-driven tactical layer with an embedded track prior, a
hysteretic FSM and a heading-aware corridor carver synthesizes a smooth,
time-varying feasible region $\mathcal{C}_k$; a SOCP local planner with sequential
linearization and a forward--backward GG-coupled velocity sweep produces
curvature-smooth, grip-respecting trajectories inside $\mathcal{C}_k$;
and an MPCC controller with a positive arc-length progress terminal and a
soft friction-circle constraint executes them at the control rate.

The simulation study shows that this separation is more than an
implementation convenience.  Across two Yas Marina fast-closure overtake
cases, the learned component changes only the feasible corridor and speed cap,
while the unchanged planner and controller complete passes with lateral RMS
tracking errors no larger than $0.05\,\mathrm{m}$ and peak speed up to
$73.10\,\mathrm{m/s}$.  These results support the central design claim:
tactical learning is most useful when it constrains an auditable model-based
stack rather than replacing it.

Future work will expand the evaluation to multi-opponent traffic, include
ablation studies of the track prior and FSM hysteresis, and transfer the
validated stack from closed-loop simulation to controlled track testing.  On
the algorithmic side, the next extensions are opponent prediction tubes in the
SOCP corridor, a conic speed-planning formulation on the GG envelope, and
tactical adaptation of the MPCC progress weight $q_{\rm prog}$.
