\title{Hierarchical Game-Guided Tactical Corridor Planning and Control for Competitive Autonomous Racing}

% \author{Shuyi Gao, Ren Jin and Defu Lin}

% \affil{School of Aerospace Engineering, Beijing Institute of Technology, Beijing, China}

\keywords{Autonomous racing, Tactical corridor planning, Overtaking decision-making.}

\begin{abstract}

High-speed autonomous racing requires an ego racecar to decide when and where
to overtake, reconstruct a feasible local driving region, and execute the
resulting trajectory near the handling limits within a narrow track corridor.
These requirements are tightly coupled: a tactically attractive pass can be
unsafe if the local corridor is not dynamically feasible, whereas a locally
feasible trajectory can be competitively ineffective if it ignores the
opponent's response and the upcoming track geometry.  This paper proposes a
hierarchical game-guided tactical corridor planning and control method for
wheel-to-wheel autonomous racing.  A Stackelberg--iterative-best-response
tactical game is formulated in the Frenet frame under track-boundary,
curvature, and tire-friction constraints, and its repeated solution is
amortized by a game-guided Truncated Quantile Critics policy.  The learned
policy does not command steering or throttle directly; instead, it generates
behavior decisions, overtaking-side selections, speed caps, and time-varying
Frenet corridors that are passed to deterministic downstream solvers.  Within
each tactical corridor, a sparse second-order cone program computes a
curvature-regularized path, a forward--backward speed profiler enforces the
GGV handling envelope, and a model predictive contouring controller tracks the
reference in closed loop.  In the high-fidelity A2RL simulator, the proposed
method achieves a 94.7\% overtaking success rate over 1000 closed-loop laps
with no observed collisions or track-boundary violations, outperforming
rule-based, non-interactive MPC, and game-theoretic baselines under the same
closed-loop execution stack.




\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =====================================================================
\IEEEPARstart{A}{utonomous} racing has become a demanding benchmark for
high-speed decision making, trajectory planning, and at-limit vehicle control.
Unlike ordinary lane-level driving, wheel-to-wheel racing compresses tactical
interaction, geometric feasibility, and vehicle dynamics into the same short
time window: the ego vehicle must infer whether an overtake is strategically
viable, reserve enough lateral space before the opportunity disappears, and
still respect tire-friction and actuator limits while executing the maneuver.
A fixed raceline tracker or a single-vehicle local planner can be fast and
stable, but it has no explicit mechanism for deciding when the nominal
raceline should be abandoned, which side should be used, or how much of the
track should be reserved as a recoverable passing corridor.  The central
problem is therefore not only to generate a collision-free trajectory, but to
construct a tactically meaningful feasible set that remains executable by the
planner and controller.




To address the coupled challenges of tactical interaction, constrained
trajectory generation, and at-limit execution, autonomous racing research has
mainly progressed along three directions: behavior planning, local motion
planning, and vehicle control. The behavior-planning layer determines
high-level racing intentions such as following, defending, and overtaking; the
local-planning layer converts these intentions into feasible trajectories
within the limited track space; and the control layer ensures accurate
execution near the handling limits of the racecar~\cite{paden2016survey,
gonzalez2016review,betz2022survey}.
At the behavior-planning layer, the ego racecar needs to determine its
high-level tactical intention, including whether to follow, shadow, defend, or
overtake, and on which side the overtaking maneuver should be attempted.
Classical autonomous driving frameworks usually rely on rule-based finite-state
machines, maneuver libraries, or hierarchical decision modules to select
discrete driving behaviors, which are subsequently converted into continuous
trajectories by the motion planner~\cite{werling2010frenet,ziegler2014baidu}.
Similar hierarchical ideas have been
adopted in autonomous racing systems, where behavior planners switch among
raceline following, obstacle avoidance, and overtaking modes according to
opponent position, track availability, and racing regulations
~\cite{betz2019architecture,kabzan2020amzracing,betz2022survey,
jung2023iac}. These rule-based methods are interpretable
and easy to integrate into real vehicles, but their tactical performance
strongly depends on manually designed thresholds and track-specific tuning.
To explicitly model the strategic coupling between competing vehicles,
game-theoretic methods formulate autonomous racing as a non-cooperative
dynamic game and compute best-response, Stackelberg, Nash-type, or
potential-game strategies~\cite{liniger2019game,wang2021game,
laine2021multi,spica2020game,jia2023bayesian,
kalaria2025alpha}. Such methods provide a principled way to describe
interactive racing behaviors, but exact equilibria of continuous dynamic games
are generally difficult to obtain in real time, and approximate solvers may be
sensitive to opponent cost assumptions and planning-horizon selection. In
parallel, reinforcement learning has shown strong capability in learning
complex racing tactics from interaction data~\cite{wurman2022gtsophy,
fuchs2021superhuman,song2021overtaking,kaufmann2023drone,lin2023limit}.
However, end-to-end policies usually lack interpretability and do not
incorporate game-theoretic interaction structures, making it difficult to
reason about competitive feasibility under track and vehicle-performance
constraints. Therefore, high-speed competitive racing still
requires a behavior-planning method that fuses game-theoretic interaction
modeling with learning-based tactical adaptation.

At the local motion-planning layer, the selected behavior or tactical intention
must be transformed into a smooth, collision-free, and dynamically feasible
trajectory within the constrained track space. In autonomous racing, many
methods are built upon offline raceline optimization, where a minimum-curvature
or minimum-time reference line is first computed from the track geometry and
vehicle dynamic limits~\cite{christ2021time,heilmeier2020mincurv,
kapania2016sequential,lipp2014minimumtime,verschueren2014nmpc}. The resulting
raceline is effective for single-vehicle lap-time optimization, but competitive
scenarios require the ego vehicle to deviate from the nominal raceline and
actively reconstruct the overtaking region when an opponent blocks the apex or
occupies the preferred racing line. To handle dynamic obstacles and alternative
passing opportunities, existing local planners employ Frenet-frame polynomial
sampling, lattice planning, graph search, trajectory libraries, homotopy-based
trajectory generation, and corridor-constrained optimization
~\cite{werling2010frenet,ziegler2014baidu,betz2019architecture,
he2021parallelized,wischnewski2023tube}. These methods can explicitly encode
track boundaries, obstacle-avoidance constraints, and part of the vehicle
dynamic constraints, which makes them suitable for real-time racing
implementation. Nevertheless, many local planners still treat the opponent
mainly as a moving obstacle and rely on the behavior planner to provide the
passing side or maneuver mode~\cite{fridovich2020ilqgames,laine2023}. As a result, the generated trajectory may be
locally collision-free but tactically conservative, especially when the
available overtaking corridor changes rapidly due to opponent motion and track
curvature. Sampling- and graph-based planners may require a large number of
candidate trajectories to cover different passing possibilities, while
nonlinear optimization methods may suffer from local minima and high
computational burden~\cite{jung2023iac,kabzan2020amzracing}. These limitations motivate a structured local-planning
scheme in which the behavior layer first constructs a time-varying tactical
corridor, and the local planner then generates a curvature-regularized path
and dynamically feasible speed profile inside this corridor~\cite{wurman2022gtsophy,fuchs2021superhuman}.

At the control layer, the planned reference must be executed accurately while
the vehicle operates close to its handling limits. Model predictive control and
model predictive contouring control are widely used in autonomous racing because
they jointly handle trajectory tracking, progress maximization, and vehicle
dynamic constraints~\cite{liniger2015optimization,liniger2019game,rosolia2020learning,
kabzan2019learning,brunner2017repetitive,novi2020realtime}; learning-based,
robust, and tube-MPC variants further improve accuracy and closed-loop
robustness under model mismatch and friction variation~\cite{rosolia2018lmpc,
hewing2020learning,wischnewski2023tube,brunke2022safelearning,he2021parallelized}.
However, the control performance depends heavily on the feasibility and
smoothness of the planned reference~\cite{betz2022software,betz2023autonomous}:
if the path ignores curvature regularity, speed feasibility, or tire-friction
limits, the controller is forced to compensate for dynamically inconsistent
commands, leading to oscillatory actuation or degraded robustness~\cite{verschueren2014nmpc}.
Behavior planning, local motion planning, and vehicle control should therefore
be coordinated within a unified hierarchical framework.




Taken together, existing studies have advanced behavior planning, local motion
planning, and vehicle control, but these modules are still weakly coupled in
high-speed competitive racing.  Behavior planners often choose a maneuver
without exposing an explicit feasible overtaking region to the local planner;
local planners can avoid a predicted opponent but usually do not encode
overtaking timing or side commitment; and controllers can only track what the
planner provides, so curvature discontinuities or speed profiles outside the
handling envelope directly degrade closed-loop robustness.  This gap motivates
a hierarchy in which the tactical layer publishes a time-varying feasible
corridor, the planner converts that corridor into a dynamically admissible
path--speed reference, and the controller executes the reference while
respecting actuator and vehicle-dynamics constraints.



This paper proposes a hierarchical game-guided tactical corridor planning and
control method for competitive autonomous racing.  The method amortizes a
Stackelberg--IBR tactical game with a Truncated Quantile Critics policy,
reconstructs the overtaking feasible region as time-varying Frenet corridors,
and combines sparse second-order cone programming, forward--backward speed
planning, and model predictive contouring control for dynamically feasible
closed-loop execution.  The main contributions are:





\begin{enumerate}
    \item A game-guided reinforcement learning method for tactical corridor
    synthesis, in which a Stackelberg--IBR tactical game provides a structured
    interaction model and structural prior, while a Truncated Quantile Critics
    policy amortizes the equilibrium solution to generate behavior decisions,
    overtaking-side selections, and time-varying Frenet tactical corridors
    under track-geometry and vehicle-performance constraints.

    \item A curvature-regularized trajectory planning method under
    tactical-corridor constraints, where a sparse second-order cone program
    generates feasible paths and a forward--backward speed strategy enforces
    vehicle performance limits, connecting feasible-region reconstruction with
    dynamically feasible trajectory generation.

    \item A model predictive contouring control method that jointly regulates
    contouring error, lag error, inputs, and vehicle dynamic constraints to
    achieve robust closed-loop tracking of the path--speed reference in complex
    overtaking scenarios.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Vehicle and Track Model}\label{sec:model}
% =====================================================================
Let $\bm r_c(s)\in\mathbb R^2$ denote the track centerline parameterized by
arc length $s\in[0,L_{\rm trk})$, where $L_{\rm trk}$ is the track length.
The centerline heading is $\theta(s)$, its curvature is
$\kappa(s)=d\theta/ds$, and its unit normal is
$\bm n_c(s)=[-\sin\theta(s),\cos\theta(s)]^\top$.  Both vehicles are described
in this common Frenet frame.  For $i\in\{e,o\}$, where $e$ and $o$ denote the
ego and opponent, respectively,
\begin{equation}
\bm p_i=\bm r_c(s_i)+n_i\bm n_c(s_i),\qquad
\xi_i=\psi_i-\theta(s_i),\qquad i\in\{e,o\}.
\label{eq:frenet_pose}
\end{equation}
Here, $\bm p_i$ and $\psi_i$ are the Cartesian position and heading of vehicle
$i$, $s_i$ is its centerline progress, $n_i$ is its signed lateral offset, and
$\xi_i$ is its heading relative to the centerline tangent.  With speed $V_i$
and yaw rate $r_i=\dot\psi_i$, the Frenet-coordinate motion is
\begin{equation}
\dot s_i=\frac{V_i\cos\xi_i}{1-\kappa(s_i)n_i},\qquad
\dot n_i=V_i\sin\xi_i,\qquad
\dot\xi_i=r_i-\kappa(s_i)\dot s_i .
\label{eq:frenet_motion}
\end{equation}
The ego--opponent relative coordinates are
\begin{equation}
\Delta s=\bigl((s_o-s_e+L_{\rm trk}/2)\bmod L_{\rm trk}\bigr)-L_{\rm trk}/2,
\qquad \Delta n=n_o-n_e,\qquad \Delta V_s=V_o-V_e .
\label{eq:rel}
\end{equation}
The wrapping in $\Delta s$ gives the signed progress difference on a closed
track.  Within one wrapping interval,
\begin{equation}
\Delta\dot s=\dot s_o-\dot s_e,\qquad
\Delta\dot n=\dot n_o-\dot n_e,\qquad
\Delta\dot V_s=\dot V_o-\dot V_e .
\label{eq:relative_motion}
\end{equation}
Figure~\ref{fig:vehicle_track_model} illustrates these coordinates.

\caption{Frenet representation of the ego--opponent interaction on a closed racetrack.}

A geometric path parameterized by $s$ is described by
$\bm z(s)=[n(s),\xi(s)]^\top$ and curvature input
$\omega(s)=\kappa_p(s)$.  Its spatial Frenet model is
\begin{equation}
\bm{z}'(s)=
\begin{bmatrix}
(1-\kappa(s)n)\tan\xi\\[2pt]
(1-\kappa(s)n)\omega/\cos\xi-\kappa(s)
\end{bmatrix},
\label{eq:spatial_model}
\end{equation}
where the prime denotes differentiation with respect to $s$.

Under pure longitudinal or pure lateral slip, the tire forces are represented
by the simplified Pacejka Magic Formula~\cite{christ2021time}
\begin{equation}
F_j(q_j,F_z)=D_j(F_z)\sin\!\left[
C_j\arctan\!\left(
B_jq_j-E_j\bigl(B_jq_j-\arctan(B_jq_j)\bigr)
\right)\right],\qquad j\in\{x,y\},
\label{eq:tire_magic_formula}
\end{equation}
where $q_x=\lambda$ and $q_y=\alpha_{\rm t}$.  The variables $\lambda$ and
$\alpha_{\rm t}$ are the longitudinal slip ratio and tire slip angle,
respectively; $F_x$ and $F_y$ are the longitudinal and lateral tire forces;
and $F_z$ is the normal load.  The load-dependent peak term is
\begin{equation}
D_j(F_z)=F_z\!\left[\mu_j+\varepsilon_j
\frac{F_z^{\rm cl}-F_{z,0}}{F_{z,0}}\right],\qquad
F_z^{\rm cl}=\operatorname{clip}\!\left(F_z,\frac{F_{z,0}}{3},3F_{z,0}\right),
\label{eq:tire_peak}
\end{equation}
where $F_{z,0}$ is the nominal load, $F_z^{\rm cl}$ is the clipped load,
$\mu_j$ is the nominal friction coefficient, and $\varepsilon_j$ is the load
sensitivity.  The coefficients $B_j$, $C_j$, and $E_j$ determine the stiffness,
shape, and curvature of the force--slip relation, respectively.  The resulting
pure-slip characteristics are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:tire_magic_formula}.

The vehicle-level acceleration boundary is represented by a speed-dependent
GGV model~\cite{rowold2023online}.  At speed $V$, let $a_T$ and $a_N$ denote
the tangential and normal accelerations.  The GGV limits are $a_N^{\max}(V)$,
$a_T^{+,\max}(V)>0$ for acceleration, and $a_T^{-,\max}(V)<0$ for braking.
Define
\begin{equation}
a_T^{\rm lim}(V,a_T)=
\begin{cases}
a_T^{+,\max}(V), & a_T\ge0,\\
|a_T^{-,\max}(V)|, & a_T<0,
\end{cases}.
\label{eq:ggv_tangent_limit}
\end{equation}
The GGV boundary is
\begin{equation}
\left|\frac{a_N}{a_N^{\max}(V)}\right|^p+
\left|\frac{a_T}{a_T^{\rm lim}(V,a_T)}\right|^p\le1,
\label{eq:ggv}
\end{equation}
where $p$ is the envelope exponent.  For path curvature $\kappa_p$,
$a_N=\kappa_pV^2$.  The signed aerodynamic acceleration is
\begin{equation}
a_{\rm drag}(V)=\frac{1}{m}\bigl(c_{d,2}V^2+c_{d,1}V+c_{d,0}\bigr),
\label{eq:drag}
\end{equation}
where $m$ is the vehicle mass and $c_{d,2}$, $c_{d,1}$, and $c_{d,0}$ are the
identified drag coefficients.

The tactical layer maps the measured ego--opponent interaction to a behavior
mode, a committed passing side, a speed cap, and a spatial corridor.  Its
game-theoretic component models the coupled maneuver, whereas the learned
policy approximates the repeated game solution and the deterministic logic

\subsection{Finite-Horizon Tactical Game}
\label{subsec:game_formulation}

The interaction is formulated as a two-player Stackelberg game with vehicle
set $\mathcal N=\{e,o\}$, where the ego is the leader and the opponent is the
follower.  Vehicle $i$ selects a hybrid tactical action
$a_i^g=(d_i,c_i)$: $d_i=(m_i,\sigma_i)$ specifies the maneuver mode and
lateral intention, and $c_i$ is a finite-dimensional parameterization of its
continuous control sequence.  The
geometric meaning of these actions is given by
Figure~\ref{fig:tactical_mode_semantics}.  For fixed $a_i^g$, the induced
control $\bm v_i(\tau;c_i)=[a_{T,i},\omega_i]^\top$ propagates
$\bm x_i=[s_i,n_i,\xi_i,V_i]^\top$ over
$\tau\in[0,T_g]$, where $T_g=N_g\Delta t_g$, according to
\begin{equation}
\dot{\bm x}_i=
\begin{bmatrix}
V_i\cos\xi_i/(1-\kappa(s_i)n_i)\\
V_i\sin\xi_i\\
V_i\omega_i-\kappa(s_i)\dot s_i\\
a_{T,i}
\end{bmatrix},\qquad i\in\{e,o\}.
\label{eq:game_dynamics}
\end{equation}
The set $\mathcal C_i(d_i)$ contains the continuous parameters whose induced
trajectory satisfies~\eqref{eq:game_dynamics}, the track boundary, the GGV
constraint~\eqref{eq:ggv}, and the control bounds; the corresponding
admissible control and state trajectories are denoted by
$\mathcal V_i(d_i)$ and $\mathcal X_i(d_i)$, respectively.  The players
also share the collision-avoidance constraint
\begin{equation}
\left(\frac{\Delta s(\tau)}{d_s}\right)^2+
\left(\frac{\Delta n(\tau)}{d_n}\right)^2\ge1,
\qquad \tau\in[0,T_g],
\label{eq:game_clearance}
\end{equation}
where $d_s$ and $d_n$ are longitudinal and lateral clearance scales.

Let $P_i=s_i(T_g)-s_i(0)$ be the unwrapped progress over the horizon and
$R_e=\Delta s(0)-\Delta s(T_g)=-R_o$ be the ego's relative progress gain.
Define the soft safety and control costs
\begin{equation}
S=\int_0^{T_g}\!\exp\!\left[-\frac{1}{2}
\left(\left(\frac{\Delta s}{d_s}\right)^2+
\left(\frac{\Delta n}{d_n}\right)^2\right)\right]d\tau,\quad
C_i=\int_0^{T_g}\!\bigl(\bm v_i^\top R_i\bm v_i+
\dot{\bm v}_i^\top S_i\dot{\bm v}_i\bigr)d\tau,
\label{eq:game_costs}
\end{equation}
where $R_i,S_i\succeq0$.  Let
$E_i^T=q_{n,i}(n_i(T_g)-n_i^{\rm tar})^2+q_{\xi,i}\xi_i^2(T_g)$,
where $n_i^{\rm tar}$ is the lateral target selected by $d_i$ and
$q_{n,i},q_{\xi,i}>0$.
The utilities of the two vehicles are explicitly
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
U_e&=w_{p,e}P_e+w_{r,e}R_e-w_{s,e}S-w_{c,e}C_e-w_{T,e}E_e^T,\\
U_o&=w_{p,o}P_o+w_{r,o}R_o-w_{s,o}S-w_{c,o}C_o-w_{T,o}E_o^T.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:utility_decomposition}
\end{equation}
Thus both vehicles seek progress and relative race advantage while retaining
clearance, smooth controls, and a recoverable terminal state.  This
progress-based construction follows the competitive receding-horizon game in
\cite{wang2021game}, with Frenet dynamics and vehicle-level constraints used
here in place of its polynomial Cartesian trajectory model.

For a fixed discrete profile $d=(d_e,d_o)$ and leader parameter $c_e$, define
the follower pseudo-gradient
\begin{equation}
G_c(c_o;d,c_e)=-\nabla_{c_o}U_o(c_o,c_e;d).
\label{eq:pseudogradient}
\end{equation}
The conditioned continuous equilibrium $\bm v_o^\star$ is characterized by
\begin{equation}
\left\langle G_c(c_o^\star;d,c_e),c_o-c_o^\star\right\rangle\ge0,
\qquad \forall c_o\in\mathcal C_o(d_o).
\label{eq:vi_equilibrium}
\end{equation}
This variational inequality is approximated by sequential IBR,
\begin{equation}
c_i^{(\nu+1)}\in
\argmax_{c_i\in\mathcal C_i(d_i)}
U_i\bigl(\bm v_i,\bm v_{-i}^{(\nu)};d\bigr),qquad i\in\{o,e\},
\label{eq:ibr_update}
\end{equation}
where $\bm v_i=\bm v_i(\cdot;c_i)$ in the utility evaluation.  Each iteration
updates the follower before the leader.  Denote its converged parameter by
$c_o^{\rm IBR}(d,c_e)$ and the induced control by
$\operatorname{BR}_o(d,\bm v_e)$.  The nominal Stackelberg control is
\begin{equation}
(d_e^\star,\bm v_e^\star)\in
\argmax_{d_e,\bm v_e:\,\bm x_e\in\mathcal X_e(d_e)}
U_e\!\left(\bm v_e,\operatorname{BR}_o(d,\bm v_e);d\right).
\label{eq:ego_tactical_decision}
\end{equation}
Finite IBR and unmodeled opponent behavior are represented by the response
neighborhood
\begin{equation}
\mathfrak R_o(d,c_e)=
\left\{c_o\in\mathcal C_o(d_o):
\|c_o-c_o^{\rm IBR}(d,c_e)\|\le\delta\right\},
\label{eq:response_neighborhood}
\end{equation}
where $\delta\ge0$ is the response radius.  The robust value of an ego
tactical candidate is
\begin{equation}
\underline{\mathcal V}_e(d,c_e)=
\min_{c_o\in\mathfrak R_o(d,c_e)}
U_e(c_e,c_o;d).
\label{eq:robust_tactical_value}
\end{equation}
Its safe set requires clearance against every such response:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal A_e^{\rm safe}(\bm o_t)=
\left\{a_e^g=(d_e,c_e):
\begin{array}{l}
c_e\in\mathcal C_e(d_e),\ \eqref{eq:game_clearance}\ \text{holds}\\
\text{for all }\tau\in[0,T_g]\text{ and }c_o\in\mathfrak R_o(d,c_e)
\end{array}\right\}.
\label{eq:safe_tactical_set}
\end{equation}
The robust Stackelberg decision is therefore
\begin{equation}
a_e^{g,\star}=(d_e^\star,c_e^\star)\in
\argmax_{a_e^g\in\mathcal A_e^{\rm safe}(\bm o_t)}
\underline{\mathcal V}_e(d,c_e).
\label{eq:robust_stackelberg_decision}
\end{equation}
If each $\mathcal C_i$ is nonempty, compact, and convex, $U_o$ is continuously
differentiable and concave in $c_o$, and $G_c$ is strongly monotone, the
conditioned equilibrium is unique.  A continuous robust value, a nonempty
compact safe set, and finitely many discrete maneuvers then ensure existence
of a leader maximizer.
Only its first tactical decision is retained before the game is reinitialized
from the next measurement.

\subsection{Game-Guided Distributional Policy}
\label{subsec:theory_guided_rl}

The policy input combines a 27-dimensional duel state with fixed map
features.  The duel state contains
\begin{equation}
\bm o_t=\bigl[
\bar V_e,\bar\xi_e,\bar n_e,\bar a_{T,e},\bar a_{N,e},\bar s_e,
\bar{\Delta s},\bar{\Delta n},\bar{\Delta V_s},\bar s_o,\bar n_o,
\bar g,\bar\tau_{\rm tc},\bar V_o,\bm b_t,
\sigma_t^{\rm lock},\bm e_{m_t}\bigr]^\top\!\in\mathbb R^{27},
\label{eq:obs}
\end{equation}
where $g=(\Delta s^2+\Delta n^2)^{1/2}$,
$\tau_{\rm tc}$ is the clipped time to contact for a closing opponent,
$\bm b_t\in\{0,1\}^3$ identifies the closing-speed regime, and
$\bm e_{m_t}\in\{0,1\}^9$ encodes the FSM state.  A bar denotes saturation
by the characteristic scale in Table~\ref{tab:setup_params}.  A fixed
300-m map query $\bm\rho(s)\in\mathbb R^{12}$ contains the current curvature,
four interval-wise maximum curvatures, curvature mean and standard deviation,
minimum and current left/right widths, and the minimum width difference.  The
actor input is therefore
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\bm o}_t=
[\bm o_t,\bm\rho(s_e),\bm\rho(s_o)]\in\mathbb R^{51}.
\label{eq:track_aug_obs}
\end{equation}

The actor produces $\bm u_t\in[-1,1]^{10}$, which is scaled and projected to
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\bm a_t&=\Pi_{\mathcal A}\!\left(\underline{\bm a}+
\tfrac12(\bm u_t+\bm1)\odot(\overline{\bm a}-\underline{\bm a})\right),\\
\bm a_t&=[L_t^{\rm cap},R_t^{\rm cap},d_{\rm safe},V_{\rm cap},
g_{\rm chase},g_{\rm ot},g_{\rm abort},b_{\rm lat},w_{\rm safe},
q_{\rm side}]^\top .
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:action_vec}
\end{equation}
The entries specify the two corridor-width caps, opponent clearance, speed
cap, three FSM gap thresholds, lateral bias, safety weight, and passing-side
score.  The projection enforces the bounds in Table~\ref{tab:setup_params},
ordered gap thresholds, and a nonzero corridor width.  The sign of
$q_{\rm side}$ selects the side and is locked during an active pass.

The game solution is mapped to the learned parameterization by
$\bm a_{\rm th}^\star=\mathcal M_g(a_e^{g,\star})$, and its supervision value
is $V_g=\underline{\mathcal V}_e(d^\star,c_e^\star)$.  The online approximation of
$\mathcal A_e^{\rm safe}$ is enforced by
\begin{equation}
\widetilde\pi_\theta(\bm u|\widetilde{\bm o}_t)\propto
\pi_\theta(\bm u|\widetilde{\bm o}_t)
\mathbf 1\!\left[
\Pi_{\mathcal A}(\bm u)\in
\widehat{\mathcal A}_e^{\rm safe}(\widetilde{\bm o}_t)\right],
\label{eq:safe_mask}
\end{equation}
where the FSM readiness gate supplies the discrete feasibility test and
$\Pi_{\mathcal A}$ enforces continuous bounds.  The game action and robust
value regularize the policy mean $\bm\mu_\theta$ and auxiliary value head
$\widehat V_\psi$ through
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L_{\rm prior}=\|\bm\mu_\theta-\bm a_{\rm th}^\star\|_{W_a}^2,
\qquad
\mathcal L_{\rm game}=(\widehat V_\psi-V_g)^2.
\label{eq:game_regularization}
\end{equation}
The environment reward is the weighted feature sum
\begin{equation}
r_t=\bm w_r^\top
[\phi_{\rm ot},\phi_{\rm ovb},\phi_{\rm col},\phi_{\rm lat},
\phi_{\rm crush},\phi_{\rm app},\phi_{\rm sm},\phi_{\rm gs},
\phi_{\rm sbs},\phi_{\rm wide}]^\top,
\label{eq:reward_sum}
\end{equation}
comprising pass, being-passed and collision events; lateral and corridor
violations; approach progress; action and corridor smoothness; safe
side-by-side motion; and usable corridor width.  Their weights are reported in
Table~\ref{tab:setup_params}.

TQC~\cite{kuznetsov2020tqc} is used as the off-policy optimizer.  Its two
$N_q$-atom critics pool their target quantiles and retain the lowest $K$ atoms
to limit value overestimation.  With the standard quantile-Huber critic loss
$J_Q$ and entropy-regularized actor loss $J_\pi$, training minimizes
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L=J_Q+\eta_\pi J_\pi+\eta_p\mathcal L_{\rm prior}
+\eta_g\mathcal L_{\rm game}.
\label{eq:total_loss}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Mode Logic and Corridor Construction}

The deterministic FSM uses
$\mathcal M=\{\textsc{Raceline},\textsc{Shadow},\textsc{Overtake},
\textsc{Hold},\textsc{Defend}\}$ as its principal racing modes.  It enters
\textsc{Shadow} when $g\le g_{\rm chase}$ and enters \textsc{Overtake} when
$g\le g_{\rm ot}$ and the selected side is wide enough over the preview at a
GGV-admissible speed.  An abort is raised when $g>g_{\rm abort}$ or the
predicted lateral clearance falls below $\Delta n_{\rm safe}$; this transfers
the mode to \textsc{Hold} and releases the side lock after the interaction.
A rear opponent within the defensive gap activates \textsc{Defend};
otherwise the mode returns to \textsc{Raceline}.  Figure~\ref{fig:tactical_mode_semantics}
shows the corresponding corridor semantics.

For the station grid $s_k=s_e+k\Delta s_{\rm sta}$, initialize
$[n_k^-,n_k^+]=[n_{\rm trk}^-(s_k),n_{\rm trk}^+(s_k)]$.  An opponent with
heading error $\xi_o$ and dimensions $(L_v,W_v)$ has projected half-extents
\begin{equation}
\rho_n=\tfrac12(L_v|\sin\xi_o|+W_v|\cos\xi_o|),\qquad
\rho_s=\tfrac12(L_v|\cos\xi_o|+W_v|\sin\xi_o|).
\label{eq:footprint_projection}
\end{equation}
With $\rho_{\rm exc}=\rho_n+W_v/2+d_{\rm safe}$ and longitudinal fade
$f_k=\cos^{p_{\rm fade}}(\pi|s_k-s_o|/(2S_{\rm fade}))$ for
$|s_k-s_o|<S_{\rm fade}$ (zero otherwise), the opponent-side bound in
\textsc{Overtake} is
\begin{equation}
\begin{cases}
n_k^-\leftarrow\max(n_k^-,n_{o,k}+\rho_{\rm exc}f_k),&\sigma_t^{\rm lock}=+1,\\
n_k^+\leftarrow\min(n_k^+,n_{o,k}-\rho_{\rm exc}f_k),&\sigma_t^{\rm lock}=-1.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:overtake_bound}
\end{equation}
The other modes apply the same projected footprint to form a preparatory
funnel, a speed-regulated hold corridor, or a defensive exclusion.  The width
caps and $b_{\rm lat}$ are blended into the far field, after which the carver
enforces $n_k^+-n_k^-\ge L_{\min}^{\rm corr}$ and applies temporal filtering
with coefficient $\beta$.

The resulting tactical output is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{G}_t=\bigl(m_{t+1},V_{{\rm cap},t},\mathcal{C}_t,\pi_t\bigr),\qquad
\mathcal{C}_t=\{(s_k,n_k^-,n_k^+)\}_{k=0}^{N_{\rm sta}-1},
\label{eq:guidance_tuple}
\end{equation}
where $\pi_t$ is the race-control permission.  Algorithm~\ref{alg:tactical}
summarizes game-guided training and the deterministic online synthesis.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Game-guided tactical corridor synthesis}
\label{alg:tactical}
\small
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Track map $\mathcal P$, action set $\mathcal A$, replay buffer $\mathcal D$
\STATE \textbf{Offline training:}
\FOR{each sampled interaction state}
  \STATE Propagate~\eqref{eq:game_dynamics} over $T_g$ and solve IBR~\eqref{eq:ibr_update} for each admissible maneuver pair
  \STATE Store $(\bm a_{\rm th}^\star,V_g)$ from the robust decision~\eqref{eq:robust_stackelberg_decision}
  \STATE Form $\widetilde{\bm o}_t$; sample $\bm u_t\sim\pi_\theta$ and project it using~\eqref{eq:action_vec}
  \STATE Update the FSM, construct $\mathcal C_t$, execute one step, and append the transition to $\mathcal D$
  \STATE Update the TQC critics and actor by~\eqref{eq:total_loss}
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{Online synthesis:}
\STATE Encode $\widetilde{\bm o}_t$ and evaluate the deterministic policy mean
\STATE Project $\bm a_t\leftarrow\Pi_{\mathcal A}(\bm\mu_\theta)$ and update $(m_{t+1},\sigma_t^{\rm lock})$
\STATE Initialize track bounds; apply mode-dependent opponent exclusion
\STATE Apply width guard and temporal filtering; publish $\mathcal G_t$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Local Planning}\label{sec:planner}
% =====================================================================
The local planner converts the tactical tuple
$\mathcal{G}_t=(m_{t+1},V_{{\rm cap},t},\mathcal{C}_t,\pi_t)$ into a
time-parameterized reference.  Its structure is deliberately split:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{G}_t
\xrightarrow{\;\text{ECOS SOCP}\;}
\{s_k,n_k^\star,\xi_k^\star,\kappa_{p,k}^\star\}_{k=0}^{N-1}
\xrightarrow{\;\text{FB speed surface}\;}
\mathcal{R}_t .
\label{eq:planner_pipeline}
\end{equation}
The first stage solves a sparse second-order cone program for the Frenet path.
The second stage computes a forward--backward (FB) speed surface on the
resulting local path using the GGV envelope of Sec.~\ref{sec:model}.

The horizon is sampled at
\begin{equation}
s_k=s_e+k\Delta s_{\rm sta},\qquad k=0,\ldots,N-1,
\label{eq:planner_grid}
\end{equation}
where $N=N_{\rm sta}$ is the number of corridor stations and the wrapped value of $s_k$ is used when querying track data.  The
tactical corridor is intersected with the physical track boundary,
\begin{equation}
\underline n_k=\max\{n_{\rm trk}^-(s_k),n_k^-\},\qquad
\overline n_k=\min\{n_{\rm trk}^+(s_k),n_k^+\}.
\label{eq:planner_corridor_intersection}
\end{equation}
If no tactical corridor is active, $\underline n_k$ and $\overline n_k$ are
the track limits after the standard safety margin.  The optimization variables
are
\begin{equation}
\bm y=
\left[
\{n_k,\xi_k,\omega_k,\sigma_k^\omega\}_{k=0}^{N-1},
\epsilon_n,\epsilon_\xi
\right],
\qquad
\bm z_k=\begin{bmatrix}n_k\\ \xi_k\end{bmatrix},
\label{eq:path_decision}
\end{equation}
where $(n_k,\xi_k)$ are the Frenet variables of Sec.~\ref{sec:model},
$\omega_k$ is the curvature-like path input in~(\ref{eq:spatial_model}),
$\sigma_k^\omega$ is the cone epigraph for $\omega_k^2$, and
$(\epsilon_n,\epsilon_\xi)$ are terminal relaxation variables.

The initial condition is hard,
\begin{equation}
\bm z_0=\begin{bmatrix}n_e\\ \xi_e\end{bmatrix},
\label{eq:path_initial}
\end{equation}
while the terminal target is mode dependent,
\begin{equation}
n_T=
\begin{cases}
n_{\rm rl}(s_{N-1}), &
m_t=\textsc{Raceline},\;
n_{\rm rl}(s_{N-1})\in[\underline n_{N-1},\overline n_{N-1}],\\[2pt]
\frac12(\underline n_{N-1}+\overline n_{N-1}), & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\qquad \xi_T=0 .
\label{eq:terminal_mode}
\end{equation}
Instead of forcing this terminal condition as an equality, the implemented
planner uses soft terminal inequalities,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
-\epsilon_n\le n_{N-1}-n_T\le \epsilon_n,\qquad
-\epsilon_\xi\le \xi_{N-1}-\xi_T\le \epsilon_\xi,\\
\epsilon_n\ge0,\qquad \epsilon_\xi\ge0 .
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:terminal_slack}
\end{equation}
This prevents ECOS infeasibility when a short horizon begins inside a tight
overtake corridor but still biases the path toward the tactical terminal
intent.

At sequential-linearization iteration $j$, the nonlinear spatial model
(\ref{eq:spatial_model}) is linearized at
$(\bm z_k^{j},\omega_k^{j})$,
\begin{equation}
\bm f_s(\bm z_k,\omega_k;s_k)
\approx
\bm A_k^j\bm z_k+\bm B_k^j\omega_k+\bm b_k^j
\triangleq \hat{\bm f}_{s,k}^j(\bm z_k,\omega_k).
\label{eq:path_affine_model}
\end{equation}
Trapezoidal integration gives the linear equality constraint
\begin{equation}
\bm z_{k+1}-\bm z_k
=\frac{\Delta s_{\rm sta}}{2}
\left[
\hat{\bm f}_{s,k}^{j}(\bm z_k,\omega_k)
+\hat{\bm f}_{s,k+1}^{j}(\bm z_{k+1},\omega_{k+1})
\right],
\quad k=0,\ldots,N-2 .
\label{eq:trap}
\end{equation}
The lateral corridor and curvature epigraph are
\begin{align}
\underline n_k\le n_k\le \overline n_k,\qquad k=0,\ldots,N-1,
\label{eq:corr_ineq}\\
\left\|
\begin{bmatrix}
2\omega_k\\ \sigma_k^\omega-1
\end{bmatrix}
\right\|_2
\le \sigma_k^\omega+1,\qquad k=0,\ldots,N-1,
\label{eq:cone}
\end{align}
which is equivalent to $(\omega_k)^2\le\sigma_k^\omega$ with
$\sigma_k^\omega\ge0$.  The SOCP solved by ECOS is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\bm y^{j+1}=\argmin_{\bm y}\;&
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} w_\omega \sigma_k^\omega
+w_{n,T}\epsilon_n+w_{\xi,T}\epsilon_\xi\\
\text{s.t.}\;&
\text{(\ref{eq:path_initial}), (\ref{eq:terminal_slack}),
(\ref{eq:trap}), (\ref{eq:corr_ineq}), and (\ref{eq:cone})}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:socp}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, ECOS receives the conic form
\begin{equation}
\min_{\bm y}\bm c^\top\bm y,\qquad
\bm A_{\rm eq}\bm y=\bm b_{\rm eq},\quad
\bm G_{\rm lp}\bm y\le\bm h_{\rm lp},\quad
\|\bm G_{q,k}\bm y+\bm g_{q,k}\|_2\le
\bm h_{q,k}^\top\bm y+h_{q,k}.
\label{eq:ecos_form}
\end{equation}
After each solve, the linearization trajectory is updated.  The loop stops
when
\begin{equation}
\Delta_n^j=\max_k|n_k^{j+1}-n_k^j|\le\varepsilon_n^{\rm sl},\qquad
\Delta_\xi^j=\max_k|\xi_k^{j+1}-\xi_k^j|\le\varepsilon_\xi^{\rm sl},
\label{eq:slin_conv}
\end{equation}
or when the iteration limit is reached.  The optimized Cartesian path and its
heading are
\begin{equation}
\bm p_k^\star=\bm r_c(s_k)+n_k^\star\bm n_c(s_k),\qquad
\psi_k^\star=\theta(s_k)+\xi_k^\star,\qquad
\kappa_{p,k}^\star\approx\omega_k^\star .
\label{eq:path_cart}
\end{equation}
The path arclength used by the speed planner is then recomputed from the
optimized Cartesian points,
\begin{equation}
\ell_0=0,\qquad
\ell_{k+1}=\ell_k+\|\bm p_{k+1}^\star-\bm p_k^\star\|_2,\qquad
\Delta\ell_k=\ell_{k+1}-\ell_k .
\label{eq:path_arclength}
\end{equation}

The FB speed profiler computes the largest speed surface that remains
consistent with curvature, longitudinal acceleration and braking.  First, a
curvature speed ceiling is obtained at each station:
\begin{equation}
V_{{\rm lim},k}=
\begin{cases}
v_{x,\max}, & |\kappa_{p,k}^\star|<\varepsilon_\kappa,\\[2pt]
\displaystyle
\lim_{r\to\infty}V_k^{(r)}, &
V_k^{(r+1)}=
\min\!\left\{
v_{x,\max},
\sqrt{\frac{a_N^{\max}(V_k^{(r)})}{|\kappa_{p,k}^\star|}}
\right\},
\end{cases}
\label{eq:vlim_iter}
\end{equation}
initialized with $V_k^{(0)}=v_{x,\max}$.  For any candidate speed $V$ at
station $k$, define
\begin{equation}
a_{N,k}(V)=|\kappa_{p,k}^\star|V^2,\qquad
\chi_k(V)=
\left[
1-\left(\frac{a_{N,k}(V)}{a_N^{\max}(V)}\right)^p
\right]_+^{1/p},
\label{eq:ggv_factor}
\end{equation}
where $[\cdot]_+=\max\{0,\cdot\}$ and $p$ is the GGV exponent in
(\ref{eq:ggv}).  The available forward and braking accelerations are
\begin{align}
a_{T,k}^{+}(V)
&=a_T^{+,\max}(V)\chi_k(V)+a_{\rm drag}(V),
\label{eq:fb_accel_available}\\
a_{T,k}^{-}(V)
&=|a_T^{-,\max}(V)|\chi_k(V)-a_{\rm drag}(V),
\label{eq:fb_brake_available}
\end{align}
where $a_T^{+,\max}>0$ and $a_T^{-,\max}<0$ are the independently calibrated
driving and braking envelopes in~(\ref{eq:ggv}); $a_{T,k}^{-}$ is the positive
deceleration magnitude used by the backward sweep.  Under the sign convention
of~(\ref{eq:drag}), $a_{\rm drag}<0$ at racing speeds, so it reduces forward
acceleration and assists braking.

The forward sweep starts from the measured speed,
\begin{equation}
V_0^f=\min\{\max(V_e,V_{\min}),V_{{\rm lim},0}\},
\label{eq:forward_init}
\end{equation}
and propagates
\begin{equation}
V_{k+1}^{f}
=
\operatorname{clip}_{[V_{\min},\,V_{{\rm lim},k+1}]}
\left(
\sqrt{(V_k^f)^2+2\Delta\ell_k\,a_{T,k}^{+}(V_k^f)}
\right),
\quad k=0,\ldots,N-2 .
\label{eq:vfwd}
\end{equation}
The backward sweep is initialized by
\begin{equation}
V_{N-1}^{b}=
\begin{cases}
\min\{V_T,V_{{\rm lim},N-1}\}, & \text{if a terminal speed is enforced},\\
\min\{V_{N-1}^{f},V_{{\rm lim},N-1}\}, & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\label{eq:backward_init}
\end{equation}
where $V_T$ is a downstream speed constraint (e.g.\ from a sharp corner
beyond the horizon or from $V_{{\rm cap},t}$).
and propagates
\begin{equation}
V_{k}^{b}
=
\operatorname{clip}_{[V_{\min},\,V_{{\rm lim},k}]}
\left(
\sqrt{(V_{k+1}^{b})^2+2\Delta\ell_k\,a_{T,k+1}^{-}(V_{k+1}^{b})}
\right),
\quad k=N-2,\ldots,0 .
\label{eq:vbwd}
\end{equation}
The final speed surface is the pointwise feasible envelope
\begin{equation}
V_k^\star=\min\{V_k^f,V_k^b,V_{{\rm lim},k},V_{{\rm cap},t}\}.
\label{eq:vmerge}
\end{equation}
The accelerations stored with the local reference are
\begin{equation}
a_{T,k}^\star=
\frac{(V_{k+1}^\star)^2-(V_k^\star)^2}{2\Delta\ell_k},\qquad
a_{N,k}^\star=\kappa_{p,k}^\star(V_k^\star)^2 .
\label{eq:atan}
\end{equation}
Finally, the time stamps are reconstructed by trapezoidal time integration,
\begin{equation}
\tau_0^r=0,\qquad
\tau_{k+1}^r=\tau_k^r+
\frac{2\Delta\ell_k}{V_k^\star+V_{k+1}^\star+\varepsilon_v},
\label{eq:time_reconstruct}
\end{equation}
and the planner publishes
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}_t=
\{(X_k^r,Y_k^r,\psi_k^r,\kappa_{p,k}^r,V_k^r,\tau_k^r)\}_{k=0}^{N_{\rm out}-1},
\label{eq:reference_tuple}
\end{equation}
after resampling the path, speed and time arrays to the controller cadence
($N_{\rm out}=N_c+1$ points at intervals $T_c$).

\section{Model Predictive Contouring Control}\label{sec:controller}
% =====================================================================
The control layer tracks the path-speed reference $\mathcal{R}_t$
from~(\ref{eq:reference_tuple}) by solving a receding-horizon nonlinear
program at the actuation rate $1/T_c$ ($T_c=T_s=0.05\,\mathrm{s}$).
A standard path-tracking MPC penalizes Cartesian deviations
$(x-x^r)^2+(y-y^r)^2$; this couples longitudinal timing to lateral
accuracy and causes aggressive re-plans when the tactical layer shifts
the corridor.  By contrast, the MPCC formulation decomposes the error into
a \emph{contouring} component (normal to the path) and a \emph{lag}
component (tangential to the path), and penalizes only the former.
Longitudinal timing is recovered through a speed-reference term, so a
corridor-side switch only changes the reference sequence without requiring
controller re-initialization.

At control instant $t$ the current vehicle pose defines the local origin.
The state is the planar pose
$\bm{\chi}_k=[x_k,y_k,\theta_k]^\top$
(with $\theta_k$ denoting yaw relative to the local frame)
and the input is the commanded speed and front-wheel steering angle
$\bm{\nu}_k=[V_k,\delta_k]^\top$.
The initial condition is
\begin{equation}
\bm{\chi}_{0|t}=\bm{0},\qquad
\bm{\nu}_{-1|t}=
\bigl[V_{-1|t},\;\delta_{-1|t}\bigr]^\top,
\label{eq:mpcc_ic}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{\nu}_{-1|t}$ is the command applied in the previous cycle,
retained for rate-constraint enforcement.
The prediction model is a kinematic bicycle with an identified nonlinear
yaw-rate map,
\begin{equation}
\bm{\chi}_{k+1|t}
=\bm{\chi}_{k|t}+T_c
\begin{bmatrix}
V_{k|t}\cos\theta_{k|t}\\[2pt]
V_{k|t}\sin\theta_{k|t}\\[2pt]
\Omega(V_{k|t},\delta_{k|t})
\end{bmatrix},
\label{eq:mpcc_model}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\Omega(V,\delta)=
\frac{\tan(a_\delta\,\delta+b_\delta)}{L_c}\,
(c_V V+d_V),
\label{eq:yaw_rate_map}
\end{equation}
where $L_c$ is the wheelbase and $\{a_\delta,b_\delta,c_V,d_V\}$ are
identified from closed-loop vehicle data using least-squares regression on
measured yaw rate, speed, and steering angle.  The model is deliberately
low-dimensional: the GGV speed profiler in Sec.~\ref{sec:planner} has
already ensured that the reference speed is dynamically feasible, so the
controller need only reject pose disturbances at the actuation rate
$1/T_c$.

The reference sequence is obtained by transforming the planner output into
the local frame:
\begin{equation}
\bar{\bm{r}}_{k|t}=
\bigl[x_{k}^{r},\;y_{k}^{r},\;\theta_{k}^{r},\;V_{k}^{r},\;
\delta_{k}^{r}\bigr]^\top,
\qquad k=1,\ldots,N_c,
\label{eq:mpcc_ref}
\end{equation}
where the reference steering is reconstructed from the planned path curvature
$\kappa_{p,k}^r$ via the inverse kinematic relation
\begin{equation}
\delta_{k}^{r}=
\arctan\!\left(\frac{L_c\,\kappa_{p,k}^r\,V_k^r}
{V_k^r+\varepsilon_v}\right).
\label{eq:steer_ff}
\end{equation}

For each prediction step, define the position error in the moving
tangent--normal frame of the reference path.  Let
$\bm{t}_k^r=[\cos\theta_k^r,\sin\theta_k^r]^\top$ and
$\bm{n}_k^r=[-\sin\theta_k^r,\cos\theta_k^r]^\top$ be the unit tangent
and normal at step $k$.  The contouring error (normal deviation), lag error
(tangential deviation), heading error, and input-tracking error are
\begin{align}
e_{c,k|t} &=(\bm{n}_{k|t}^{r})^\top
\bigl([x_{k|t}-x_{k}^{r},\;y_{k|t}-y_{k}^{r}]^\top\bigr),
\label{eq:mpcc_ec}\\
e_{\ell,k|t} &=(\bm{t}_{k|t}^{r})^\top
\bigl([x_{k|t}-x_{k}^{r},\;y_{k|t}-y_{k}^{r}]^\top\bigr),
\label{eq:mpcc_el}\\
e_{\psi,k|t} &=\mathrm{wrap}(\theta_{k|t}-\theta_{k}^{r}),
\label{eq:mpcc_epsi}\\
\bm{e}_{\nu,k|t} &=\bm{\nu}_{k|t}-\bm{\nu}_{k}^{r}.
\label{eq:mpcc_enu}
\end{align}
The contouring error $e_c$ measures the lateral deviation from the planned
path and is the primary tracking objective.  The lag error $e_\ell$ is not
penalized in the cost; longitudinal timing is handled implicitly through
the speed reference in $\bm{e}_\nu$.  This choice avoids forcing the
vehicle to chase replanned time-stamps when the tactical layer shifts the
corridor.

The stage cost and the complete optimization problem are
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\min_{\mathcal{U}_t}\;\;&
q_{\Delta\delta}\bigl(\delta_{0|t}-\delta_{-1|t}\bigr)^2
+\sum_{k=0}^{N_c-1}\!
\Bigl[
q_c\, e_{c,k+1|t}^2
+q_\psi\, e_{\psi,k+1|t}^2
+\bm{e}_{\nu,k|t}^\top R_\nu\,\bm{e}_{\nu,k|t}
\Bigr]\\[3pt]
\mathrm{s.t.}\;\;&
\bm{\chi}_{0|t}=\bm{0},\\
&\bm{\chi}_{k+1|t}
=f_d^{\rm kin}(\bm{\chi}_{k|t},\bm{\nu}_{k|t}),
\quad k=0,\ldots,N_c{-}1,\\
&V_{\min}\le V_{k|t}\le v_{x,\max},\\
&|\delta_{k|t}|\le\delta_{\max},\\
&|V_{k|t}-V_{k-1|t}|\le\Delta V_{\max},\\
&|\delta_{k|t}-\delta_{k-1|t}|\le\dot\delta_{\max}\,T_c,
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:mpcc_nlp}
\end{equation}
where $R_\nu=\mathrm{diag}(r_V,r_\delta)$, and
$f_d^{\rm kin}(\cdot)$ denotes the kinematic discrete dynamics
in~(\ref{eq:mpcc_model})--(\ref{eq:yaw_rate_map}).
The weight $q_{\Delta\delta}$ penalizes the first steering increment to
enforce actuator continuity across cycles.  The speed and steering rate
constraints prevent saturation and ensure smooth transitions during
corridor-side switches initiated by the tactical layer.

The NLP is solved in condensed form with $2N_c$ decision variables by a
warm-started interior-point method.  The receding-horizon policy applies
only $(V_{0|t}^\star,\delta_{0|t}^\star)$ and re-solves at the next cycle.
The actuator mapping converts this pair into the physical command vector
\begin{equation}
\bm{u}_{\rm act}=
\mathcal{M}(V_{0|t}^\star,\delta_{0|t}^\star)
=\bigl[T_{\rm cmd},\;P_{b,\rm cmd},\;S_{\rm cmd},\;g_{\rm cmd}\bigr]^\top,
\label{eq:actuator_map}
\end{equation}
where $T_{\rm cmd}\in[0,100]$ is the throttle percentage,
$P_{b,\rm cmd}\in[0,P_{b,\max}]$ is the brake pressure,
$S_{\rm cmd}\in[S_{\min},S_{\max}]$ is the steering actuator position, and
$g_{\rm cmd}\in\{1,\ldots,G\}$ is the gear.  The longitudinal force
request driving the throttle/brake split is
\begin{equation}
F_x^{\rm req}=
\underbrace{c_{d,2}V^2+c_{d,1}V+c_{d,0}
+m\frac{V_{\rm la}-V_{0|t}^\star}{T_{\rm la}}}_{F_{\rm ff}}
+\underbrace{K_p^V(V_{0|t}^\star-V_{\rm meas})
+K_i^V I_V}_{F_{\rm fb}},
\label{eq:force_req}
\end{equation}
where $V=V_{\rm meas}$ is the current measured speed,
$V_{\rm la}$ is a look-ahead reference speed interpolated $T_{\rm la}$
seconds ahead on the speed profile, $I_V$ is the integrator state of the
speed error, and $K_p^V$, $K_i^V$ are PI gains.
The allocation rule is
\begin{equation}
T_{\rm cmd}=
\begin{cases}
\min\bigl\{100\,F_x^{\rm req}/F_{\max}(\omega_e,g),\;
T_{\rm tc}\bigr\},
& F_x^{\rm req}>0,\\[3pt]
0, & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\label{eq:throttle_map}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
P_{b,\rm cmd}=
\begin{cases}
0, & F_x^{\rm req}>0,\\[3pt]
\mathrm{clip}\!\left(
\dfrac{-F_x^{\rm req}\,r_w}{2k_b},\;0,\;P_{b,\max}\right),
& \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\label{eq:brake_map}
\end{equation}
where $T_{\rm tc}$ is the traction-control limit and $k_b$ converts
pressure to wheel torque.  The steering actuator is mapped as
$S_{\rm cmd}=r_s(\delta_{0|t}^\star+\delta_{\rm off})$
with calibrated ratio $r_s$ and offset $\delta_{\rm off}$.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% =====================================================================
This paper presented a hierarchical game-guided tactical corridor planning and
control architecture for wheel-to-wheel autonomous racing.  The framework
formulates tactical interaction as a Stackelberg--IBR game and amortizes its
repeated solution with a game-guided Truncated Quantile Critics policy.  The
learned component publishes behavior modes, side commitments, speed caps, and
Frenet corridor parameters, while the deterministic FSM and corridor carver
convert these outputs into auditable spatial constraints.  Inside the corridor,
a sparse SOCP planner generates curvature-regularized paths, a
forward--backward speed sweep enforces the GGV handling envelope, and an MPCC
executes the resulting path--speed reference in closed loop.

The A2RL high-fidelity simulation study shows that this division of labor is
effective for short high-speed passes, prolonged cornering duels, and
equal-speed adversarial engagements.  Across 1000 closed-loop evaluation laps,
the full architecture achieved a 94.7\% overtaking success rate with no
observed collisions or track-boundary violations, and the comparison and
ablation studies indicate that both the game-guided policy structure and the
learned corridor parameters are necessary for the reported safety and
performance.  The results therefore support the main design principle: learning
should shape the tactical feasible set, while model-based planning and control
retain responsibility for geometric consistency, handling-limit speed
selection, and actuator-feasible execution.  Future work will extend the
framework to multi-opponent races, incorporate probabilistic opponent
prediction into the corridor constraints, and validate the sim-to-track
transfer on controlled Dallara EAV24 tests.
