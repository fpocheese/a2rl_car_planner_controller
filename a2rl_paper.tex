\title{Hierarchical Game-Guided Tactical Corridor Planning and Control for Competitive Autonomous Racing}

\author{Shuyi Gao, Ren Jin and Defu Lin}

\affil{School of Aerospace Engineering, Beijing Institute of Technology, Beijing, China}

\keywords{Autonomous racing, Tactical corridor planning, Overtaking decision-making.}

\begin{abstract}

Autonomous racecars in high-speed competitive racing must jointly perform overtaking decision 
making, trajectory reconstruction, and at-limit control within a confined track space. 
To address the challenges of highly dynamic vehicle motion, stringent track constraints, 
complex interactive competition, and low overtaking success rates caused by uncertain feasible 
overtaking opportunities, this paper proposes a hierarchical game-guided tactical corridor 
planning and control method. First, a game-based competitive situation model is established 
in the track Frenet frame under racecar performance-limit constraints to characterize the 
feasible motion space subject to track boundaries, curvature variations, and tire-friction limits. 
Then, the game-theoretic interaction model is amortized by a Truncated Quantile Critics
reinforcement learning policy at the tactical layer, which adaptively generates behavior
decisions, overtaking-side selections, and time-varying Frenet tactical corridors. Within the tactical corridor, a sparse second-order 
cone program is employed to generate curvature-regularized paths, while forward--backward speed 
planning and model predictive contouring control are integrated to achieve dynamically feasible 
trajectory generation and closed-loop tracking. High-fidelity simulation results show 
that the proposed method can adaptively reconstruct the overtaking feasible region according 
to opponent position, track geometry, and vehicle performance boundaries, achieving a 94.7% 
overtaking success rate over 1000 closed-loop laps.




\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =====================================================================
\IEEEPARstart{W}{ith} the rapid development of autonomous driving technologies and 
artificial intelligence methods, autonomous racing has become an important platform 
for validating high-speed decision making, trajectory planning, and at-limit control 
of intelligent vehicles. Enhancing the autonomous competitive capability of racecars 
in adversarial racing scenarios is therefore of great significance. However, high-speed 
racing is characterized by highly dynamic vehicle motion, strongly constrained track space, 
and complex interactive competition among vehicles. Under such conditions, merely relying 
on fixed raceline tracking or single-vehicle trajectory planning makes it difficult to 
accurately identify feasible overtaking opportunities and achieve stable overtaking. 
Therefore, developing a hierarchical planning and control method that integrates 
game-theoretic decision making, trajectory reconstruction, and vehicle dynamic constraints 
has become a key issue for improving the strategic performance of autonomous racecars in 
high-speed competitive racing.




To address the coupled challenges of tactical interaction, constrained
trajectory generation, and at-limit execution, autonomous racing research has
mainly progressed along three directions: behavior planning, local motion
planning, and vehicle control. The behavior-planning layer determines
high-level racing intentions such as following, defending, and overtaking; the
local-planning layer converts these intentions into feasible trajectories
within the limited track space; and the control layer ensures accurate
execution near the handling limits of the racecar~\cite{paden2016survey,
gonzalez2016review,betz2022survey}.
At the behavior-planning layer, the ego racecar needs to determine its
high-level tactical intention, including whether to follow, shadow, defend, or
overtake, and on which side the overtaking maneuver should be attempted.
Classical autonomous driving frameworks usually rely on rule-based finite-state
machines, maneuver libraries, or hierarchical decision modules to select
discrete driving behaviors, which are subsequently converted into continuous
trajectories by the motion planner~\cite{werling2010frenet,ziegler2014baidu}.
Similar hierarchical ideas have been
adopted in autonomous racing systems, where behavior planners switch among
raceline following, obstacle avoidance, and overtaking modes according to
opponent position, track availability, and racing regulations
~\cite{betz2019architecture,kabzan2020amzracing,betz2022survey,
jung2023iac}. These rule-based methods are interpretable
and easy to integrate into real vehicles, but their tactical performance
strongly depends on manually designed thresholds and track-specific tuning.
To explicitly model the strategic coupling between competing vehicles,
game-theoretic methods formulate autonomous racing as a non-cooperative
dynamic game and compute best-response, Stackelberg, Nash-type, or
potential-game strategies~\cite{liniger2019game,wang2021game,
laine2021multi,spica2020game,jia2023bayesian,
kalaria2025alpha}. Such methods provide a principled way to describe
interactive racing behaviors, but exact equilibria of continuous dynamic games
are generally difficult to obtain in real time, and approximate solvers may be
sensitive to opponent cost assumptions and planning-horizon selection. In
parallel, reinforcement learning has shown strong capability in learning
complex racing tactics from interaction data~\cite{wurman2022gtsophy,
fuchs2021superhuman,song2021overtaking,kaufmann2023drone,lin2023limit}.
However, end-to-end policies usually lack interpretability and do not
incorporate game-theoretic interaction structures, making it difficult to
reason about competitive feasibility under track and vehicle-performance
constraints. Therefore, high-speed competitive racing still
requires a behavior-planning method that fuses game-theoretic interaction
modeling with learning-based tactical adaptation.

At the local motion-planning layer, the selected behavior or tactical intention
must be transformed into a smooth, collision-free, and dynamically feasible
trajectory within the constrained track space. In autonomous racing, many
methods are built upon offline raceline optimization, where a minimum-curvature
or minimum-time reference line is first computed from the track geometry and
vehicle dynamic limits~\cite{christ2021time,heilmeier2020mincurv,
kapania2016sequential,lipp2014minimumtime,verschueren2014nmpc}. The resulting
raceline is effective for single-vehicle lap-time optimization, but competitive
scenarios require the ego vehicle to deviate from the nominal raceline and
actively reconstruct the overtaking region when an opponent blocks the apex or
occupies the preferred racing line. To handle dynamic obstacles and alternative
passing opportunities, existing local planners employ Frenet-frame polynomial
sampling, lattice planning, graph search, trajectory libraries, homotopy-based
trajectory generation, and corridor-constrained optimization
~\cite{werling2010frenet,ziegler2014baidu,betz2019architecture,
he2021parallelized,wischnewski2023tube}. These methods can explicitly encode
track boundaries, obstacle-avoidance constraints, and part of the vehicle
dynamic constraints, which makes them suitable for real-time racing
implementation. Nevertheless, many local planners still treat the opponent
mainly as a moving obstacle and rely on the behavior planner to provide the
passing side or maneuver mode~\cite{fridovich2020ilqgames,laine2023}. As a result, the generated trajectory may be
locally collision-free but tactically conservative, especially when the
available overtaking corridor changes rapidly due to opponent motion and track
curvature. Sampling- and graph-based planners may require a large number of
candidate trajectories to cover different passing possibilities, while
nonlinear optimization methods may suffer from local minima and high
computational burden~\cite{jung2023iac,kabzan2020amzracing}. These limitations motivate a structured local-planning
scheme in which the behavior layer first constructs a time-varying tactical
corridor, and the local planner then generates a curvature-regularized path
and dynamically feasible speed profile inside this corridor~\cite{wurman2022gtsophy,fuchs2021superhuman}.

At the control layer, the planned reference must be executed accurately while
the vehicle operates close to its handling limits. Model predictive control and
model predictive contouring control are widely used in autonomous racing because
they jointly handle trajectory tracking, progress maximization, and vehicle
dynamic constraints~\cite{liniger2015optimization,liniger2019game,rosolia2020learning,
kabzan2019learning,brunner2017repetitive,novi2020realtime}; learning-based,
robust, and tube-MPC variants further improve accuracy and closed-loop
robustness under model mismatch and friction variation~\cite{rosolia2018lmpc,
hewing2020learning,wischnewski2023tube,brunke2022safelearning,he2021parallelized}.
However, the control performance depends heavily on the feasibility and
smoothness of the planned reference~\cite{betz2022software,betz2023autonomous}:
if the path ignores curvature regularity, speed feasibility, or tire-friction
limits, the controller is forced to compensate for dynamically inconsistent
commands, leading to oscillatory actuation or degraded robustness~\cite{verschueren2014nmpc}.
Behavior planning, local motion planning, and vehicle control should therefore
be coordinated within a unified hierarchical framework.




Taken together, existing studies have advanced behavior planning, local motion
planning, and vehicle control individually, but these modules remain
insufficiently coordinated in high-speed competitive racing: behavior planners
lack an explicit representation of the feasible overtaking space, local planners
have limited tactical modeling of opponent interaction and overtaking timing,
and controllers depend on the curvature continuity and tire-friction consistency
of the planned reference. It is therefore of great significance to develop a
unified hierarchical method that integrates tactical decision making,
overtaking feasible-region reconstruction, dynamically feasible trajectory
generation, and closed-loop execution, enabling the ego racecar to adaptively
reconstruct the overtaking space according to opponent motion, track geometry,
and vehicle performance boundaries.



This paper addresses the autonomous racing demand of high-speed competitive
autonomous racing, and proposes a hierarchical game-guided tactical corridor
planning and control method to solve the problems of overtaking-timing
selection, feasible overtaking-space reconstruction, and weak coordination
between planning and control under complex interactive racing scenarios. The
proposed method amortizes game-theoretic equilibrium solutions via a Truncated
Quantile Critics reinforcement learning policy for tactical decision making,
constructs an overtaking feasible-region reconstruction mechanism based on
time-varying Frenet tactical corridors, and integrates sparse second-order cone
programming, forward--backward speed planning, and model predictive contouring
control to achieve dynamically feasible trajectory generation and closed-loop
execution.
The proposed hierarchical game-guided tactical corridor planning and control
method has the following advantages:





\begin{enumerate}
    \item A game-guided reinforcement learning method for tactical corridor synthesis, in which a Stackelberg--IBR tactical game provides a structured interaction model and theory prior, while a Truncated Quantile Critics policy amortizes the equilibrium solution to adaptively generate behavior decisions, overtaking-side selections, and time-varying Frenet tactical corridors under track-geometry and vehicle-performance constraints.

    \item A curvature-regularized trajectory planning method under tactical-corridor constraints, where a sparse second-order cone program generates feasible paths and a forward--backward speed strategy enforces vehicle performance limits, connecting feasible-region reconstruction with dynamically feasible trajectory generation.

    \item A model predictive contouring control method that jointly regulates contouring error, lag error, inputs, and vehicle dynamic constraints to achieve robust closed-loop tracking of the path--speed reference in complex overtaking scenarios.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Vehicle, Track and GGV Modeling}\label{sec:model}
% =====================================================================
% All subsequent layers use the same two descriptions of the vehicle state.  The
% controller uses a dynamic bicycle model in an inertial frame, because tire
% slip, yaw dynamics and actuator limits must be enforced at the control rate.
% The tactical and planning layers use the Frenet coordinates of the same
% vehicle with respect to the track centerline, because overtaking decisions are

\subsection{Dynamic Bicycle Model}
Let the continuous-time controller state and input be
\begin{equation}
\bm{x}=\bigl[X,\;Y,\;\psi,\;v_x,\;v_y,\;r,\;\delta,\;s_p\bigr]^\top,\qquad
\bm{u}=\bigl[a,\;\dot\delta\bigr]^\top ,
\label{eq:state}
\end{equation}
where $(X,Y)$ is the inertial position, $\psi$ is yaw angle, $(v_x,v_y)$ are
body-frame longitudinal and lateral velocities, $r$ is yaw rate, $\delta$ is
front steering angle, and $s_p$ is an auxiliary progress state used only by
the MPCC terminal reward.  The input $a$ is the commanded longitudinal
acceleration and $\dot\delta$ is the steering-rate command.  The rigid-body
kinematics are
\begin{equation}
\dot X=v_x\cos\psi-v_y\sin\psi,\quad
\dot Y=v_x\sin\psi+v_y\cos\psi,\quad
\dot\psi=r,\quad \dot s_p=v_x .
\label{eq:kin}
\end{equation}
The front and rear slip angles are
\begin{align}
\alpha_f &= \arctan\!\left(\frac{v_y+l_f r}{v_x+\varepsilon_v}\right)-\delta,
\label{eq:alphaf}\\
\alpha_r &= \arctan\!\left(\frac{v_y-l_r r}{v_x+\varepsilon_v}\right),
\label{eq:alphar}
\end{align}
where the small positive $\varepsilon_v$ regularizes the low-speed
denominator.  With linear lateral tire stiffness,
\begin{equation}
F_{yf}=-C_f\alpha_f,\qquad F_{yr}=-C_r\alpha_r .
\label{eq:tire}
\end{equation}
The resulting Newton--Euler equations are
\begin{align}
\dot v_x &= a+r v_y-\frac{1}{m}F_{yf}\sin\delta,
\label{eq:vxdot}\\
\dot v_y &= \frac{1}{m}\bigl(F_{yf}\cos\delta+F_{yr}\bigr)-r v_x,
\label{eq:vydot}\\
\dot r &= \frac{1}{I_z}\bigl(l_fF_{yf}\cos\delta-l_rF_{yr}\bigr).
\label{eq:rdot}
\end{align}
The steering angle evolves as $\dot\delta=u_\delta$ where $u_\delta$ is the
steering-rate input in~(\ref{eq:state}).
Equations~(\ref{eq:kin})--(\ref{eq:rdot}) define
\begin{equation}
\dot{\bm{x}}=f_c(\bm{x},\bm{u}),\qquad
\bm{x}_{k+1}=f_d(\bm{x}_k,\bm{u}_k),
\label{eq:fd}
\end{equation}
where $f_d$ is the fourth-order Runge--Kutta discretization with sampling
time $T_s$.  Numerical parameter values are collected in
Table~\ref{tab:setup_params}.

\subsection{Track Frenet Coordinates}
Let $\bm{r}_c(s)\in\R^2$ be the track centerline parameterized by arc length
$s\in[0,L_{\rm trk})$.  Its tangent angle is $\theta(s)$ and its curvature is
$\kappa(s)=d\theta/ds$.  Define the tangent and normal unit vectors as
\begin{equation}
\bm{t}(s)=\bigl[\cos\theta(s),\;\sin\theta(s)\bigr]^\top,\qquad
\bm{n}_c(s)=\bigl[-\sin\theta(s),\;\cos\theta(s)\bigr]^\top .
\label{eq:frenet_frame}
\end{equation}
Any vehicle pose sufficiently close to the track is represented by
\begin{equation}
\bm{p}=\begin{bmatrix}X\\Y\end{bmatrix}
=\bm{r}_c(s)+n\,\bm{n}_c(s),\qquad
\xi=\psi-\theta(s),
\label{eq:frenet_pose}
\end{equation}
where $n$ is the signed lateral offset and $\xi$ is the heading error.  The
body velocity projected onto the Frenet axes is
\begin{equation}
v_s=v_x\cos\xi-v_y\sin\xi,\qquad
v_n=v_x\sin\xi+v_y\cos\xi .
\label{eq:frenet_velocity}
\end{equation}
Differentiating~(\ref{eq:frenet_pose}) gives the exact kinematic relation
\begin{align}
\dot s &= \frac{v_s}{1-\kappa(s)n},
\label{eq:sdot_frenet}\\
\dot n &= v_n,
\label{eq:ndot_frenet}\\
\dot\xi &= r-\kappa(s)\dot s .
\label{eq:xidot_frenet}
\end{align}
The wrapped progress difference used in tactical reasoning is always computed
on this same coordinate,
\begin{equation}
\Delta s=\bigl((s_o-s_e+L_{\rm trk}/2)\bmod L_{\rm trk}\bigr)-L_{\rm trk}/2,
\qquad \Delta n=n_o-n_e .
\label{eq:rel}
\end{equation}

For the path planner it is more convenient to use $s$ as the independent
variable and to describe a path by $\bm{z}(s)=[n(s),\xi(s)]^\top$.  Let
$\omega(s)$ denote the curvature-like steering variable of this geometric
path.  The spatial Frenet model is
\begin{equation}
\bm{z}'(s)=
\bm{f}_s\bigl(\bm{z}(s),\omega(s);s\bigr)
=
\begin{bmatrix}
(1-\kappa n)\tan\xi\\[2pt]
(1-\kappa n)\omega/\cos\xi-\kappa
\end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:spatial_model}
\end{equation}
At planning iteration $j$, this model is linearized about the previous path
iterate $(\bar{\bm{z}}_k,\bar\omega_k)$ at each station $s_k$,
\begin{equation}
\bm{z}'(s_k)\approx
\bm{A}_k\bm{z}_k+\bm{B}_k\omega_k+\bm{b}_k,
\label{eq:linear_spatial_model}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{A}_k$, $\bm{B}_k$ and $\bm{b}_k$ are the Jacobian and affine
residual of $\bm{f}_s$.  This single linearized Frenet model is the one used
by the SOCP in Sec.~\ref{sec:planner}; no new lateral or heading variables are
introduced later.

\subsection{GGV Handling Envelope}
The speed planner and MPCC share the same handling-limit interpretation.  For
a speed $V$, the identified GGV table returns the available tangential and
normal acceleration limits $a_T^{\max}(V)$ and $a_N^{\max}(V)$.  Combined slip
is approximated by the super-elliptic envelope
\begin{equation}
\left|\frac{a_N}{a_N^{\max}(V)}\right|^p+
\left|\frac{a_T}{a_T^{\max}(V)}\right|^p\le1,
\label{eq:ggv}
\end{equation}
with exponent $p=0.75$.  Given a path curvature $\kappa_p$ and speed $V$, the
normal acceleration is $a_N=\kappa_pV^2$, and the remaining tangential
acceleration is
\begin{equation}
a_T^{\rm avail}(V,a_N)=a_T^{\max}(V)
\left[1-\left(\frac{|a_N|}{a_N^{\max}(V)}\right)^p\right]^{1/p}.
\label{eq:ggv_tangent}
\end{equation}
Aerodynamic drag is modeled as
\begin{equation}
a_{\rm drag}(V)=\frac{1}{m}\bigl(c_{d,2}V^2+c_{d,1}V+c_{d,0}\bigr),
\label{eq:drag}
\end{equation}
and is added with the acceleration or braking sign convention stated in
Sec.~\ref{sec:planner}.  The envelope~(\ref{eq:ggv}) is not a reward term; it
is a physical admissibility model used by both the speed profiler and the
controller.

\section{Tactical Corridor Synthesis}\label{sec:tactical}
% =====================================================================
The tactical layer is a game-guided learned decision module with a
deterministic optimization interface.  Its design is motivated by a
Stackelberg--IBR tactical game
(Sec.~\ref{subsec:game_formulation}) whose repeated online solution is
amortized by a theory-guided TQC policy
(Sec.~\ref{subsec:theory_guided_rl}).  The policy outputs a
low-dimensional parameter vector encoding the behavior mode, overtaking side,
and corridor-shaping parameters, from which a deterministic carver constructs
the time-varying Frenet corridor.
Let $\mathcal{B}(s)=[n_{\rm trk}^-(s),n_{\rm trk}^+(s)]$
denote the track boundary in the Frenet coordinates of Sec.~\ref{sec:model},
and let $\mathcal{O}_t$ denote the observed opponent set.  Online operation is
written as the composition
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t
&=\mathcal{E}\bigl(\bm{x}_{e,t},\mathcal{O}_t,\mathcal{P},
m_t,\sigma_t^{\rm lock}\bigr),\\
\bm{u}_t&\sim\pi_\theta(\cdot|\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t),\\
\bm{a}_t&=\Pi_{\mathcal{A}}\!\left(
\underline{\bm{a}}+\frac{\bm{u}_t+\bm{1}}{2}\odot
(\overline{\bm{a}}-\underline{\bm{a}})\right),\\
m_{t+1}&=\mathcal{F}_{m}(\bm{o}_t,\bm{a}_t,m_t,\sigma_t^{\rm lock}),\\
\mathcal{C}_t&=\mathcal{F}_{c}(\mathcal{B},\mathcal{O}_t,\bm{a}_t,
m_{t+1},\sigma_t^{\rm lock}),\\
\mathcal{G}_t&=(m_{t+1},V_{{\rm cap},t},\mathcal{C}_t,\pi_t).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:tactical_stack}
\end{equation}
Only $\pi_\theta$ is learned.  The projection $\Pi_{\mathcal A}$, the
finite-state machine $\mathcal{F}_m$, and the corridor carver
$\mathcal{F}_c$ are deterministic maps that convert the policy output into
auditable constraints for the planner.  The training problem is
\begin{equation}
\pi_\theta^\star=
\operatorname*{arg\,max}_{\pi_\theta}
\mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}\!
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^{T_{\rm ep}-1}\gamma^t
r\bigl(\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t,\bm{a}_t,m_t,\mathcal{C}_t\bigr)
\right],
\label{eq:tactical_objective}
\end{equation}
so reinforcement learning is used to select a tactical feasible set rather
than low-level steering or throttle.

Before presenting the RL implementation, the next two subsections establish the
game-theoretic model that motivates the policy structure and supplies its
theory-guided prior.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Stackelberg Tactical Game Formulation}
\label{subsec:game_formulation}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------

The corridor-synthesis problem is fundamentally interactive: the tactical
value of a candidate maneuver depends not only on the currently visible free
space, but also on the response it induces in surrounding vehicles and on the
terminal planning quality it leaves for subsequent receding-horizon cycles.
Therefore, the tactical layer is modeled as a leader--follower (Stackelberg)
game in which the ego vehicle (leader) proposes a hybrid tactical action and
the surrounding vehicles (followers) respond through a constrained continuous
subgame.

Let $\mathcal{N}=\{1,\dots,N_v\}$ denote the vehicle set and $e\in\mathcal{N}$
the ego.  Each vehicle $i\in\mathcal{N}$ selects a hybrid tactical action
\begin{equation}
a_i=(d_i,c_i),\qquad d_i=(m_i,\ell_i),\qquad c_i=(\alpha_i,\rho_i),
\label{eq:hybrid_action_game}
\end{equation}
where $d_i$ is the discrete component (mode $m_i$ and lateral intention
$\ell_i$) and $c_i$ is the continuous component (aggressiveness $\alpha_i$ and
preference $\rho_i$).  The tactical utility of vehicle $i$ is decomposed as
\begin{equation}
U_i
=
U_i^{\mathrm{prog}}
+U_i^{\mathrm{race}}
+U_i^{\mathrm{safe}}
+U_i^{\mathrm{term}}
+U_i^{\mathrm{ctrl}},
\label{eq:utility_decomposition}
\end{equation}
instantiated for the ego as
\begin{align}
U_e^{\mathrm{prog}} &= w_p\,\Delta s_e,
\label{eq:ego_prog}\\
U_e^{\mathrm{race}} &= w_r\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_{-e}}
                      (\Delta s_{ej}^{k+H}-\Delta s_{ej}^k),
\label{eq:ego_race}\\
U_e^{\mathrm{safe}} &= -w_s\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_{-e}}
                      \phi_s(\Delta_{ej},\tau_{ej}),
\label{eq:ego_safe}\\
U_e^{\mathrm{term}} &= w_t\,\Phi_{\mathrm{rec}}(x_e^N,\mathcal{R}_e),
\label{eq:ego_term}\\
U_e^{\mathrm{ctrl}} &= -w_c\,\|a_e^k-a_e^{k-1}\|^2,
\label{eq:ego_ctrl}
\end{align}
where $\Delta_{ej}$ is an interaction-sensitive separation metric,
$\tau_{ej}$ a severity indicator, and
$\Phi_{\mathrm{rec}}$ measures the recursive planning quality of
the terminal state delivered to the next local OCP cycle.
Each follower $i\in\mathcal{N}_{-e}$ maximizes an analogous utility
\begin{equation}
U_i = w_i^p\Phi_i^p + w_i^r\Phi_i^r - w_i^s\Phi_i^s
      + w_i^t\Phi_i^t - w_i^c\Phi_i^c.
\label{eq:follower_utility}
\end{equation}

Conditioning on a fixed discrete profile $d=(d_e,d_{-e})$, the followers
interact over the continuous variables
$c_{-e}=\mathrm{col}(c_i)_{i\in\mathcal{N}_{-e}}$ through the constrained
response
\begin{equation}
\max_{c_i\in\mathcal{C}_i(d_i)} U_i,\qquad
\mathcal{C}_i(d_i)=\{c_i\mid g_i\le0,\;h_i=0\}.
\label{eq:follower_response}
\end{equation}
Define the pseudo-gradient mapping
\begin{equation}
G_c(c_{-e};d,c_e)
=\mathrm{col}\!\bigl(
-\nabla_{c_i}U_i
\bigr)_{i\in\mathcal{N}_{-e}}.
\label{eq:pseudogradient}
\end{equation}
A conditioned continuous follower equilibrium $c_{-e}^\star$ satisfies the
variational inequality (VI)
\begin{equation}
\bigl\langle
G_c(c_{-e}^\star;d,c_e),\;c_{-e}-c_{-e}^\star
\bigr\rangle\ge0,
\quad\forall\,c_{-e}\in\mathcal{C}_{-e}(d_{-e}),
\label{eq:vi_equilibrium}
\end{equation}
with $\mathcal{C}_{-e}=\prod_{i\in\mathcal{N}_{-e}}\mathcal{C}_i$.
Because the tactical layer must remain synchronous with the
receding-horizon planner, an iterative best-response (IBR) approximation
replaces the VI solver:
\begin{equation}
c_i^{(\nu+1)}\in
\arg\max_{c_i\in\mathcal{C}_i(d_i)}
U_i\bigl(z_i,d,c_e,c_i,c_{-i}^{(\nu)}\bigr),
\label{eq:ibr_update}
\end{equation}
yielding, at convergence,
$c_{-e}^{\mathrm{IBR}\star}(d,c_e)=\lim_{\nu\to\infty}c_{-e}^{(\nu)}$.

To guard against fragile single-response predictions, a local response
neighborhood is defined as
\begin{equation}
\mathfrak{R}_{-e}(d,c_e)=
\left\{c_{-e}\in\mathcal{C}_{-e}
\;\middle|\;
\|c_{-e}-c_{-e}^{\mathrm{IBR}\star}\|\le\delta\right\},
\label{eq:response_neighborhood}
\end{equation}
and the robust tactical value is
\begin{equation}
\underline{\mathcal{V}}_e(d,c_e)=
\min_{c_{-e}\in\mathfrak{R}_{-e}(d,c_e)}
U_e\!\bigl((d_e,c_e),(d_{-e},c_{-e})\bigr).
\label{eq:robust_tactical_value}
\end{equation}
The safe tactical set
$\mathcal{A}_e^{\mathrm{safe}}(o_k)\subseteq\mathcal{A}_e$ excludes
tactically unsafe or planner-destructive actions.  The upper-layer decision
is therefore
\begin{equation}
a_e^{k,\star}\in
\arg\max_{(d_e,c_e)\in\mathcal{A}_e^{\mathrm{safe}}(o_k)}
\underline{\mathcal{V}}_e(d,c_e).
\label{eq:ego_tactical_decision}
\end{equation}
The existence of a well-defined tactical decision follows from the strong
monotonicity of $G_c$ on the compact convex follower set and the Weierstrass
theorem on the compact safe action set.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Theory-Guided RL Approximation of the Tactical Equilibrium}
\label{subsec:theory_guided_rl}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------

Exact online evaluation of~\eqref{eq:ego_tactical_decision} is
computationally expensive because each candidate action requires follower
IBR convergence and terminal quality assessment.  The role of the TQC policy
$\pi_\theta$ in~\eqref{eq:tactical_stack} is therefore to \emph{amortize}
repeated solutions of~\eqref{eq:ego_tactical_decision} while preserving its
strategic semantics.  Concretely, the Stackelberg--IBR game supplies three
structural priors to the learning problem:

\paragraph{Game-induced reward decomposition.}
The RL reward mirrors the utility decomposition~\eqref{eq:utility_decomposition}
so that its gradient landscape remains consistent with the upper-layer game.

\paragraph{Theory-guided prior policy.}
The game induces a structured prior over the continuous tactical parameter
space.  Denote the IBR-consistent tactical parameters by
$\bm{a}_{\mathrm{th}}^\star(\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t)$.  This prior regularizes
the learned policy mean through a weighted penalty:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{prior}}=
\bigl\|\mu_\theta(\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t)
-\bm{a}_{\mathrm{th}}^\star(\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t)\bigr\|_{W_c}^2,
\label{eq:prior_mse}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_\theta$ is the deterministic policy mean and $W_c$ is a
diagonal weighting matrix that emphasizes safety-critical components
(gap thresholds, clearance) over preference parameters (lateral bias).

\paragraph{Auxiliary game-value head.}
An auxiliary critic head
$\hat{\mathcal{G}}_\psi(\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t,\bm{a}_t)$ is trained to
approximate the robust tactical value:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{game}}=
\bigl(\hat{\mathcal{G}}_\psi(\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t,\bm{a}_t)
-\underline{\mathcal{V}}_e(\bm{a}_t\mid\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t)\bigr)^2.
\label{eq:game_value_loss}
\end{equation}
This head improves credit assignment and keeps the learned Q-value anchored
to the interaction-aware tactical game, rather than drifting toward an
experience-biased value estimate.

\paragraph{Safe masking.}
In the game formulation, $\mathcal{A}_e^{\mathrm{safe}}(o_k)$ excludes
inadmissible tactical actions.  In the implementation, this is realized through
the projection $\Pi_{\mathcal{A}}$ in~\eqref{eq:safety_clip} and the
readiness gate of the FSM: a mode transition to \textsc{Overtake} is blocked
unless the corridor-width and GGV-speed admissibility conditions are
simultaneously satisfied.  Formally, the effective safe policy satisfies
\begin{equation}
\tilde\pi_\theta(\bm{u}\mid\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t)\propto
\pi_\theta(\bm{u}\mid\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t)\,
\mathbf{1}\!\bigl[\Pi_{\mathcal{A}}(\bm{u})\in
\widehat{\mathcal{A}}_e^{\mathrm{safe}}(\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t)\bigr],
\label{eq:safe_mask}
\end{equation}
which eliminates exploration of corridor parameters that would yield
infeasible or collision-prone FSM transitions.

The overall TQC training objective combines the standard distributional
Bellman loss with the game-derived regularizers:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}=
J_Q(\varphi)
+\lambda_\pi J_\pi(\theta)
+\lambda_p\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{prior}}
+\lambda_g\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{game}},
\label{eq:total_loss}
\end{equation}
where $J_Q$ and $J_\pi$ are the quantile-Huber critic loss and the entropy-regularized actor loss defined below.
The learning problem therefore remains structurally anchored to the
Stackelberg--IBR tactical game: TQC amortizes the equilibrium computation,
the game supplies the structural prior, and safe masking preserves
admissibility---so that the resulting corridor policy is simultaneously
data-efficient, interaction-aware, and planner-compatible.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Observation Encoding and Map-Conditioned Features}
\label{subsec:obs_encoding}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------

The remaining subsections instantiate the abstract game quantities into
concrete policy variables.  The tactical step index $k$ used in the game
formulation is hereafter written as $t$ to emphasize real-time operation.
The abstract observation $o_k$ is realized as
$\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t$; the discrete component $(m_t,\sigma_t)$
corresponds to the FSM mode and committed pass side; and the continuous
component $c_t$ is implemented by the ten-dimensional parameter vector
$\bm{a}_t$ through the tanh mapping and projection
$\Pi_{\mathcal{A}}$ defined below.

The encoder $\mathcal{E}$ combines a duel-state observation with
map-conditioned features that provide curvature, width, and
vehicle-capability context.  The dynamic observation is
\begin{align}
\bm{o}_t^{\rm ego}
&=\bigl[
\bar V_e,\;\bar\xi_e,\;\bar n_e,\;\bar a_{T,e},\;\bar a_{N,e},\;\bar s_e
\bigr]^\top,
\label{eq:obs_ego}\\
\bm{o}_t^{\rm opp}
&=\bigl[
\bar{\Delta s},\;\bar{\Delta n},\;\bar{\Delta V_s},\;\bar s_o,\;\bar n_o,\;
\bar g,\;\bar\tau_{\rm tc},\;\bar V_o
\bigr]^\top ,
\label{eq:obs_opp}\\
\bm{o}_t
&=\bigl[
\bm{o}_t^{\rm ego},\;\bm{o}_t^{\rm opp},\;\bm{b}_t,\;
\sigma_t^{\rm lock},\;\bm{e}_{m_t}
\bigr].
\label{eq:obs}
\end{align}
With five FSM modes, $\bm{o}_t\in\mathbb{R}^{23}$.  Each scalar is normalized
as
\begin{equation}
\bar z=\operatorname{sat}(z/z_{\rm char}),\qquad
\operatorname{sat}(q)=\min\{1,\max\{-1,q\}\},
\label{eq:normalization}
\end{equation}
using the characteristic values in Table~\ref{tab:setup_params}.  The
relative progress and lateral separation are given by~(\ref{eq:rel}),
$\Delta V_s=V_o-V_e$, and $g=\sqrt{\Delta s^2+\Delta n^2}$.  The clipped
time-to-collision feature is
\begin{equation}
\tau_{\rm tc}=
\begin{cases}
\operatorname{clip}\!\left(\Delta s/\max(-\Delta V_s,\varepsilon_\tau),
0,\tau_{\rm tc,\max}\right), & \Delta s>0,\ \Delta V_s<0,\\
\tau_{\rm tc,\max}, & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\label{eq:ttc}
\end{equation}
so only a closing leader creates a finite urgency scale.  The bucket vector
$\bm{b}_t$ coarsely encodes whether the ego is fast-closing, speed-matched or
receding.  The memory variables $\sigma_t^{\rm lock}$ and $\bm{e}_{m_t}$ make
side commitment and hysteresis observable to the policy.

The dynamic duel state is augmented by a non-trainable map
$\mathcal{P}=\{\bm{\rho}_i\}_{i=0}^{M-1}$.  This map contains not only
curvature and width, but also a local estimate of vehicle capability.  From
the GGV envelope in Sec.~\ref{sec:model}, define the curvature-limited speed
preview
\begin{equation}
V_{\rm ggv}(s)=
\sup_{0\le V\le v_{x,\max}}
\left\{
V:\; |\kappa(s)|V^2\le a_N^{\max}(V)
\right\},
\label{eq:ggv_preview}
\end{equation}
with $V_{\rm ggv}=v_{x,\max}$ when the curvature constraint is inactive.
For a preview interval
$\mathcal I(s;d_a,d_b)=[s+d_a,s+d_b]$, the statistics inserted into the
policy are
\begin{equation}
\kappa_{d_a:d_b}^{\max}(s)=
\max_{\ell\in\mathcal I(s;d_a,d_b)}|\kappa(\ell)|,\quad
W_{\min}^{L/R}(s)=
\min_{\ell\in[s,s+d_p]} |n_{\rm trk}^{+/-}(\ell)|,\quad
V_{\min}^{\rm ggv}(s)=
\min_{\ell\in[s,s+d_p]}V_{\rm ggv}(\ell).
\label{eq:prior_statistics}
\end{equation}
Querying $\mathcal{P}$ at progress $s$ returns a 14-dimensional vector
\begin{equation}
\bm{\rho}(s)=
\bigl[
\underbrace{\bar\kappa_0,\;
\bar\kappa_{0:d_1}^{\max},\ldots,
\bar\kappa_{d_3:d_p}^{\max},\;
\bar\kappa_{\rm mean},\;
\bar\kappa_{\rm std}}_{\text{curvature preview (7)}},\;
\underbrace{\bar W_{\min}^{L},\;
\bar W_{\min}^{R},\;
\bar W_0^L,\;
\bar W_0^R,\;
\bar{\Delta W}_{\min}}_{\text{width (5)}},\;
\underbrace{\bar V_0^{\rm ggv},\;
\bar V_{\min}^{\rm ggv}}_{\text{GGV (2)}}
\bigr]^\top\!.
\label{eq:track_prior}
\end{equation}
The curvature terms encode upcoming cornering severity at four look-ahead
intervals, the width terms encode whether an overtake side remains
geometrically open, and the GGV terms encode whether the vehicle can
exploit that side without exceeding lateral-acceleration capability.  The
final actor--critic input is
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t=
\bigl[\bm{o}_t,\bm{\rho}(s_e),\bm{\rho}(s_o)\bigr]\in\mathbb{R}^{51},
\label{eq:track_aug_obs}
\end{equation}
so an identical relative gap can yield different actions on a straight, at
corner entry, or before a narrow exit.

The actor output is not a trajectory but a ten-dimensional tactical parameter
vector.  A sample $\bm{u}_t\in[-1,1]^{10}$ is mapped to
\begin{equation}
\bm{a}_t=\Pi_{\mathcal A}\!\left(
\underline{\bm{a}}+
\frac{1}{2}(\bm{u}_t+\bm{1})\odot(\overline{\bm{a}}-\underline{\bm{a}})
\right),
\label{eq:tanh_map}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\bm{a}_t=
\bigl[
L_t^{\rm cap},R_t^{\rm cap},d_{\rm safe},V_{\rm cap},
g_{\rm chase},g_{\rm ot},g_{\rm abort},b_{\rm lat},w_{\rm safe},q_{\rm side}
\bigr]^\top .
\label{eq:action_vec}
\end{equation}
The projection $\Pi_{\mathcal A}$ enforces
\begin{equation}
\mathcal A=\left\{
\bm{a}:\;
g_{\rm chase}\ge g_{\rm ot}+\varepsilon_{co},\;
g_{\rm abort}\ge g_{\rm chase}+\varepsilon_{ca},\;
L_t^{\rm cap}+R_t^{\rm cap}\ge\varepsilon_{\rm corr}
\right\}.
\label{eq:safety_clip}
\end{equation}
The components of $\bm{a}_t$ have direct downstream meanings:
$(L_t^{\rm cap},R_t^{\rm cap})$ cap the left/right corridor width,
$d_{\rm safe}$ sets opponent clearance, $V_{\rm cap}$ caps the speed profile,
$(g_{\rm chase},g_{\rm ot},g_{\rm abort})$ tune FSM hysteresis, $b_{\rm lat}$
sets the far-field lateral bias, $w_{\rm safe}$ controls recovery width, and
$q_{\rm side}$ chooses the pass side,
\begin{equation}
\sigma_t=
\begin{cases}
+1, & q_{\rm side}\ge0\quad \text{left-side pass},\\
-1, & q_{\rm side}<0\quad \text{right-side pass}.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:side_sign}
\end{equation}
Once $\sigma_t$ is accepted in \textsc{Overtake}, it is copied into
$\sigma_t^{\rm lock}$ and held until the duel terminates.

The continuous tactical action is converted into a persistent intention by a
hysteretic finite-state machine with modes
$\mathcal{M}=\{\textsc{Raceline},\textsc{Shadow},\textsc{Overtake},
\textsc{Hold},\textsc{Defend}\}$.  Let $g_{\rm rear}$ denote the closest
rear gap and $\bar c_t$ indicate that the post-overtake immunity window has
expired.  The selected side is considered ready when the previewed side width
and GGV speed are both admissible,
\begin{equation}
{\rm ready}_t(\sigma)=
\mathbb{1}\!\left[
\min_{s\in[s_e,s_e+d_p]} W_\sigma(s) \ge L_{\min}^{\rm corr}
\right]
\mathbb{1}\!\left[
\min_{s\in[s_e,s_e+d_p]} V_{\rm ggv}(s) \ge \min\{V_e,V_{\rm cap}\}
\right],
\label{eq:ready}
\end{equation}
where $W_{+1}$ and $W_{-1}$ are the left/right widths remaining after
opponent exclusion.  The mode update is
\begin{equation}
m_{t+1}=
\begin{cases}
\textsc{Overtake}, &
g\le g_{\rm ot}\wedge {\rm ready}_t(\sigma_t)\wedge\bar c_t,\\
\textsc{Shadow}, &
g_{\rm ot}<g\le g_{\rm chase},\\
\textsc{Hold}, &
m_t=\textsc{Overtake}\wedge{\rm abort}_t,\\
\textsc{Defend}, &
g>g_{\rm def}^{\rm front}\wedge0<g_{\rm rear}<g_{\rm def}^{\rm rear}\wedge\bar c_t,\\
\textsc{Raceline}, &
g>g_{\rm chase},\\
m_t, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:fsm}
\end{equation}
The abort flag is
\begin{equation}
{\rm abort}_t=
\bigl(g>g_{\rm abort}\bigr)\vee
\bigl(\Delta n_{\min}<\Delta n_{\rm safe}\wedge |\Delta s|<g_{\rm interlock}\bigr),
\label{eq:abort}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta n_{\min}$ is the minimum lateral separation predicted within
the local interaction window.  Therefore the policy adjusts thresholds and
side preference, while the hybrid logic preserves a persistent mode sequence
for the downstream optimizer.

The green shaded area denotes the feasible corridor published by the tactical layer, and the green curves denote its lateral boundaries.  \textsc{Raceline} and \textsc{Hold} share the same full-track-width corridor; the former encourages lap-time-oriented driving, whereas the latter keeps the corridor open but regulates the speed profile through $V_{{\rm cap},t}$ to maintain a safe gap to the preceding vehicle.  \textsc{Shadow}, \textsc{Overtake}, and \textsc{Defend} further reshape the corridor according to the opponent position, respectively guiding the ego to the rear-side of the opponent on the chosen passing side in preparation for the pass, supporting side-specific passing, and blocking the opponent's forward driving direction.

The reward is defined on tactical outcome and constraint quality.  At each
step,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
r_t={}&
w_{\rm ot}\phi_{\rm ot}
+w_{\rm ovb}\phi_{\rm ovb}
+w_{\rm col}\phi_{\rm col}
+w_{\rm lat}\phi_{\rm lat}
+w_{\rm crush}\phi_{\rm crush}
+w_{\rm app}\phi_{\rm app}\\
&+
w_{\rm sm}\phi_{\rm sm}
+w_{\rm gs}\phi_{\rm gs}
+w_{\rm sbs}\phi_{\rm sbs}
+w_{\rm wide}\phi_{\rm wide},
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:reward_sum}
\end{equation}
with weights listed in Table~\ref{tab:setup_params}.  The event and geometry
features are
\begin{align}
\phi_{\rm ot}
&=\mathbb{1}[\Delta s_{t-1}>0]\mathbb{1}[\Delta s_t<0]\mathbb{1}[\bar c_t],
\label{eq:reward_pass}\\
\phi_{\rm ovb}
&=\mathbb{1}[\Delta s_{t-1}<0]\mathbb{1}[\Delta s_t>0],
\label{eq:reward_overtaken}\\
\phi_{\rm col}
&=\mathbb{1}[{\rm contact}\vee{\rm offtrack}\vee{\rm comm\_loss}],
\label{eq:reward_collision}\\
\phi_{\rm lat}
&=\mathbb{1}[m_t=\textsc{Raceline}]
\left(\frac{n_e-n_{\rm rl}(s_e)}{n_{\rm char}}\right)^2,
\label{eq:reward_lateral}\\
\phi_{\rm crush}
&=\mathbb{1}[|\Delta s|<g_{\rm interlock}]
\left(\frac{\max\{0,\Delta n_{\rm safe}-|\Delta n|\}}{n_{\rm char}}\right)^2 .
\label{eq:reward_crush}
\end{align}
The approach feature is
\begin{equation}
\phi_{\rm app}=
\mathbb{1}[\Delta s>0]\,
\mathbb{1}[g_t<g_{\rm chase}]\,
\operatorname{clip}\!\left(\frac{g_{t-1}-g_t}{g_{\rm char}},0,1\right),
\label{eq:approach_reward}
\end{equation}
and the regularizers applied to the policy output and the published corridor
are
\begin{equation}
\phi_{\rm sm}=
\|\bm{a}_t-\bm{a}_{t-1}\|_{\bm{W}_a}^2,
\label{eq:action_smooth_reward}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\phi_{\rm gs}=
\frac{1}{N_{\rm sta}}\sum_{k=0}^{N_{\rm sta}-1}
\Bigl[
(n_{k,t}^+-n_{k,t-1}^+)^2+
(n_{k,t}^--n_{k,t-1}^-)^2
\Bigr],
\label{eq:corridor_smooth_reward}
\end{equation}
\begin{align}
\phi_{\rm sbs}
&=\mathbb{1}[|\Delta s|<g_{\rm interlock}]
\mathbb{1}[|\Delta n|\ge\Delta n_{\rm safe}],
\label{eq:reward_sbs}\\
\phi_{\rm wide}
&=\frac{1}{N_{\rm sta}}\sum_{k=0}^{N_{\rm sta}-1}
\operatorname{clip}\!\left(
\frac{n_k^+-n_k^- -L_{\min}^{\rm corr}}{n_{\rm char}},0,1
\right).
\label{eq:reward_wide}
\end{align}
Positive weights reward pass completion, useful gap closing, safe
side-by-side tenure and non-degenerate corridors.  Negative weights penalize
being overtaken, collision, unnecessary raceline deviation, lateral
compression, action jitter and corridor jitter.  Hence the reward shapes only
the tactical interface; it does not modify the SOCP or MPCC objectives.
This reward construction preserves the same strategic decomposition that
defines the tactical utility~\eqref{eq:utility_decomposition}, ensuring
consistency between the game-derived prior and the learning signal.

\subsection{Distributional Policy Optimization with TQC}
\label{subsec:tqc_optimization}

The policy is optimized with Truncated Quantile Critics (TQC), a
distributional off-policy algorithm chosen specifically because its
pessimistic value estimate reduces the risk of committing to corridors
that appear advantageous in expectation but carry high collision
probability in the tail---a property validated by the ablation study in
Sec.~\ref{sec:experiments} where replacing TQC with standard SAC increases
collisions from zero to five.  Two critics parameterized by $\varphi_i$
approximate the return distribution with $N_q$ quantile atoms
$\{z_{\varphi_i}^{j}(\widetilde{\bm{o}},\bm{u})\}_{j=1}^{N_q}$.  Given
a replay transition, draw
$\bm{u}'\sim\pi_\theta(\cdot|\widetilde{\bm{o}}')$ and form the truncated
target
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{Z}_{\rm targ}=
\operatorname{sort}\Bigl(
\{r_t+\gamma(1-d_t)
[z_{\bar\varphi_i}^{j}(\widetilde{\bm{o}}',\bm{u}')
-\alpha\log\pi_\theta(\bm{u}'|\widetilde{\bm{o}}')]\}_{i,j}
\Bigr)_{1:K}.
\label{eq:tqc_target}
\end{equation}
Retaining only the lowest $K$ out of $2N_q$ atoms suppresses
overestimation of rare high-reward overtakes that coincide with near-miss
geometries.  The critic minimizes the quantile Huber loss
\begin{equation}
J_Q(\varphi)=
\sum_{i=1}^{2}\sum_{j=1}^{N_q}
\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal D}
\left[
\rho_{\hat\tau_j}^{\kappa_q}
\left(
y-z_{\varphi_i}^{j}(\widetilde{\bm{o}}_t,\bm{u}_t)
\right)
\right],
\label{eq:tqc_critic}
\end{equation}
where $y\in\mathcal{Z}_{\rm targ}$, and the actor maximizes the truncated
Q-value with entropy regularization:
\begin{equation}
J_\pi(\theta)=
\mathbb{E}_{\widetilde{\bm{o}}\sim\mathcal D,\bm{u}\sim\pi_\theta}
\left[
\alpha\log\pi_\theta(\bm{u}|\widetilde{\bm{o}})
-\frac{1}{K}\sum_{z\in\mathcal{Z}_{K}(\widetilde{\bm{o}},\bm{u})}z
\right].
\label{eq:tqc_actor}
\end{equation}
The entropy temperature $\alpha$ is tuned automatically.
Together with the game-derived
regularizers~\eqref{eq:prior_mse}--\eqref{eq:game_value_loss}, the combined
objective~\eqref{eq:total_loss} ensures that the learned policy remains a
structurally faithful approximation of the Stackelberg--IBR equilibrium.
Training episodes
randomize initial gap, relative speed, lateral offset and opponent behavior;
termination occurs at pass completion, being overtaken, collision, off-track
excursion, communication loss or timeout.  Evaluation uses the deterministic
mean action with the same FSM and corridor carver.

The tactical output consumed by the planner is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{G}_t=\bigl(m_t,V_{{\rm cap},t},\mathcal{C}_t,\pi_t\bigr),\qquad
\mathcal{C}_t=\{(s_k,n_k^-,n_k^+)\}_{k=0}^{N_{\rm sta}-1},
\label{eq:guidance_tuple}
\end{equation}
where $\pi_t$ stores race-control permission.  The carver is the deterministic
map
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}_t=
\mathcal{F}_{c}
\left(
\{n_{\rm trk}^-(s_k),n_{\rm trk}^+(s_k)\}_{k=0}^{N_{\rm sta}-1},
\mathcal{O}_t,m_t,\sigma_t^{\rm lock},\bm{a}_t
\right).
\label{eq:carver_map}
\end{equation}
It first initializes the station grid and physical bounds,
\begin{equation}
s_k=s_e+k\Delta s_{\rm sta},\qquad
n_k^-=n_{\rm trk}^-(s_k),\qquad
n_k^+=n_{\rm trk}^+(s_k).
\label{eq:corridor_init}
\end{equation}
For an opponent with heading error $\xi_o$ and footprint $(L_v,W_v)$, the
half-extents projected onto Frenet axes are
\begin{equation}
\rho_n=\frac{1}{2}(L_v|\sin\xi_o|+W_v|\cos\xi_o|),\qquad
\rho_s=\frac{1}{2}(L_v|\cos\xi_o|+W_v|\sin\xi_o|),
\label{eq:footprint_projection}
\end{equation}
and the lateral exclusion radius is
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\rm exc}=\rho_n+\frac{W_v}{2}+d_{\rm safe}.
\label{eq:rho_exc}
\end{equation}
The longitudinal influence of the footprint is
\begin{equation}
\phi_{\rm fade}(\Delta s_k)=
\begin{cases}
\cos^{p_{\rm fade}}\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\frac{|\Delta s_k|}{S_{\rm fade}}\right),
& |\Delta s_k|<S_{\rm fade},\\
0, & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\label{eq:fade}
\end{equation}
multiplied by a startup ramp $\phi_{\rm sr}(k)$ and a distance ramp
$\phi_{\rm rmp}(k)$.  In \textsc{Overtake}, the bound on the opponent side is
tightened and the selected side is left open:
\begin{equation}
\begin{cases}
n_k^- \leftarrow
\max\!\left(n_k^-,\, n_{o,k}+\rho_{\rm exc}\phi_{\rm fade}\phi_{\rm sr}\phi_{\rm rmp}\right),
& \sigma_t=+1,\\[3pt]
n_k^+ \leftarrow
\min\!\left(n_k^+,\, n_{o,k}-\rho_{\rm exc}\phi_{\rm fade}\phi_{\rm sr}\phi_{\rm rmp}\right),
& \sigma_t=-1.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:overtake_bound}
\end{equation}
In \textsc{Shadow}, the interval is shaped into an asymmetric funnel toward
$\sigma_t$; in \textsc{Hold}, it is relaxed toward the raceline; in
\textsc{Defend}, it is biased toward the defensive side.  The learned bias
and width caps are then blended into the far field.  With
\begin{equation}
w_k=
\begin{cases}
(k/k_{\rm blend})^2, & k<k_{\rm blend},\\
1, & k\ge k_{\rm blend},
\end{cases}
\label{eq:rl_blend_w}
\end{equation}
\begin{align}
n_k^+ &\leftarrow (1-w_k)n_k^+
+w_k\min\bigl(n_k^+,b_{\rm lat}+L_t^{\rm cap}\bigr),
\label{eq:rl_blend_max}\\
n_k^- &\leftarrow (1-w_k)n_k^-
+w_k\max\bigl(n_k^-,b_{\rm lat}-R_t^{\rm cap}\bigr).
\label{eq:rl_blend_min}
\end{align}
The minimum-width guard is
\begin{equation}
n_k^+-n_k^- \ge L_{\min}^{\rm corr}.
\label{eq:min_corridor_width}
\end{equation}
If this inequality cannot be recovered within the physical track boundary,
the previous feasible interval is held at that station.  Finally, the
published corridor is temporally filtered,
\begin{equation}
\begin{bmatrix}n_k^-\\n_k^+\end{bmatrix}_{t}
\leftarrow
\beta
\begin{bmatrix}n_k^-\\n_k^+\end{bmatrix}_{t}
+(1-\beta)
\begin{bmatrix}n_k^-\\n_k^+\end{bmatrix}_{t-1},
\label{eq:ema}
\end{equation}
and the tuple $\mathcal{G}_t$ is sent to the SOCP planner.  Thus the learned
layer contributes exactly three planning quantities: $m_t$ selects the
mode-dependent terminal target, $V_{{\rm cap},t}$ clips the GGV speed profile,
and $\mathcal{C}_t$ supplies the lateral inequalities
$n_k^-\le n_k\le n_k^+$ in Sec.~\ref{sec:planner}.  If a tactical update is
delayed by the simulation interface, the last valid $\mathcal{G}_t$ is
held for one planning cycle.

% =====================================================================
\section{Local Planning}\label{sec:planner}
% =====================================================================
The local planner converts the tactical tuple
$\mathcal{G}_t=(m_t,V_{{\rm cap},t},\mathcal{C}_t,\pi_t)$ into a
time-parameterized reference.  Its structure is deliberately split:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{G}_t
\xrightarrow{\;\text{ECOS SOCP}\;}
\{s_k,n_k^\star,\xi_k^\star,\kappa_{p,k}^\star\}_{k=0}^{N-1}
\xrightarrow{\;\text{FB speed surface}\;}
\mathcal{R}_t .
\label{eq:planner_pipeline}
\end{equation}
The first stage solves a sparse second-order cone program for the Frenet path.
The second stage computes a forward--backward (FB) speed surface on the
resulting local path using the GGV envelope of Sec.~\ref{sec:model}.

The horizon is sampled at
\begin{equation}
s_k=s_e+k\Delta s_{\rm sta},\qquad k=0,\ldots,N-1,
\label{eq:planner_grid}
\end{equation}
where the wrapped value of $s_k$ is used when querying track data.  The
tactical corridor is intersected with the physical track boundary,
\begin{equation}
\underline n_k=\max\{n_{\rm trk}^-(s_k),n_k^-\},\qquad
\overline n_k=\min\{n_{\rm trk}^+(s_k),n_k^+\}.
\label{eq:planner_corridor_intersection}
\end{equation}
If no tactical corridor is active, $\underline n_k$ and $\overline n_k$ are
the track limits after the standard safety margin.  The optimization variables
are
\begin{equation}
\bm y=
\left[
\{n_k,\xi_k,\omega_k,\sigma_k^\omega\}_{k=0}^{N-1},
\epsilon_n,\epsilon_\xi
\right],
\qquad
\bm z_k=\begin{bmatrix}n_k\\ \xi_k\end{bmatrix},
\label{eq:path_decision}
\end{equation}
where $(n_k,\xi_k)$ are the Frenet variables of Sec.~\ref{sec:model},
$\omega_k$ is the curvature-like path input in~(\ref{eq:spatial_model}),
$\sigma_k^\omega$ is the cone epigraph for $\omega_k^2$, and
$(\epsilon_n,\epsilon_\xi)$ are terminal relaxation variables.

The initial condition is hard,
\begin{equation}
\bm z_0=\begin{bmatrix}n_e\\ \xi_e\end{bmatrix},
\label{eq:path_initial}
\end{equation}
while the terminal target is mode dependent,
\begin{equation}
n_T=
\begin{cases}
n_{\rm rl}(s_{N-1}), &
m_t=\textsc{Raceline},\;
n_{\rm rl}(s_{N-1})\in[\underline n_{N-1},\overline n_{N-1}],\\[2pt]
\frac12(\underline n_{N-1}+\overline n_{N-1}), & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\qquad \xi_T=0 .
\label{eq:terminal_mode}
\end{equation}
Instead of forcing this terminal condition as an equality, the implemented
planner uses soft terminal inequalities,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
-\epsilon_n\le n_{N-1}-n_T\le \epsilon_n,\qquad
-\epsilon_\xi\le \xi_{N-1}-\xi_T\le \epsilon_\xi,\\
\epsilon_n\ge0,\qquad \epsilon_\xi\ge0 .
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:terminal_slack}
\end{equation}
This prevents ECOS infeasibility when a short horizon begins inside a tight
overtake corridor but still biases the path toward the tactical terminal
intent.

At sequential-linearization iteration $j$, the nonlinear spatial model
(\ref{eq:spatial_model}) is linearized at
$(\bm z_k^{j},\omega_k^{j})$,
\begin{equation}
\bm f_s(\bm z_k,\omega_k;s_k)
\approx
\bm A_k^j\bm z_k+\bm B_k^j\omega_k+\bm b_k^j
\triangleq \hat{\bm f}_{s,k}^j(\bm z_k,\omega_k).
\label{eq:path_affine_model}
\end{equation}
Trapezoidal integration gives the linear equality constraint
\begin{equation}
\bm z_{k+1}-\bm z_k
=\frac{\Delta s_k}{2}
\left[
\hat{\bm f}_{s,k}^{j}(\bm z_k,\omega_k)
+\hat{\bm f}_{s,k+1}^{j}(\bm z_{k+1},\omega_{k+1})
\right],
\quad k=0,\ldots,N-2 .
\label{eq:trap}
\end{equation}
The lateral corridor and curvature epigraph are
\begin{align}
\underline n_k\le n_k\le \overline n_k,\qquad k=0,\ldots,N-1,
\label{eq:corr_ineq}\\
\left\|
\begin{bmatrix}
2\omega_k\\ \sigma_k^\omega-1
\end{bmatrix}
\right\|_2
\le \sigma_k^\omega+1,\qquad k=0,\ldots,N-1,
\label{eq:cone}
\end{align}
which is equivalent to $(\omega_k)^2\le\sigma_k^\omega$ with
$\sigma_k^\omega\ge0$.  The SOCP solved by ECOS is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\bm y^{j+1}=\argmin_{\bm y}\;&
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} w_\omega \sigma_k^\omega
+w_{n,T}\epsilon_n+w_{\xi,T}\epsilon_\xi\\
\text{s.t.}\;&
\text{(\ref{eq:path_initial}), (\ref{eq:terminal_slack}),
(\ref{eq:trap}), (\ref{eq:corr_ineq}), and (\ref{eq:cone})}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:socp}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, ECOS receives the conic form
\begin{equation}
\min_{\bm y}\bm c^\top\bm y,\qquad
\bm A_{\rm eq}\bm y=\bm b_{\rm eq},\quad
\bm G_{\rm lp}\bm y\le\bm h_{\rm lp},\quad
\|\bm G_{q,k}\bm y+\bm g_{q,k}\|_2\le
\bm h_{q,k}^\top\bm y+h_{q,k}.
\label{eq:ecos_form}
\end{equation}
After each solve, the linearization trajectory is updated.  The loop stops
when
\begin{equation}
\Delta_n^j=\max_k|n_k^{j+1}-n_k^j|\le\varepsilon_n^{\rm sl},\qquad
\Delta_\xi^j=\max_k|\xi_k^{j+1}-\xi_k^j|\le\varepsilon_\xi^{\rm sl},
\label{eq:slin_conv}
\end{equation}
or when the iteration limit is reached.  The optimized Cartesian path and its
heading are
\begin{equation}
\bm p_k^\star=\bm r_c(s_k)+n_k^\star\bm n_c(s_k),\qquad
\psi_k^\star=\theta(s_k)+\xi_k^\star,\qquad
\kappa_{p,k}^\star=\omega_k^\star .
\label{eq:path_cart}
\end{equation}
The path arclength used by the speed planner is then recomputed from the
optimized Cartesian points,
\begin{equation}
\ell_0=0,\qquad
\ell_{k+1}=\ell_k+\|\bm p_{k+1}^\star-\bm p_k^\star\|_2,\qquad
\Delta\ell_k=\ell_{k+1}-\ell_k .
\label{eq:path_arclength}
\end{equation}

The FB speed profiler computes the largest speed surface that remains
consistent with curvature, longitudinal acceleration and braking.  First, a
curvature speed ceiling is obtained at each station:
\begin{equation}
V_{{\rm lim},k}=
\begin{cases}
v_{x,\max}, & |\kappa_{p,k}^\star|<\varepsilon_\kappa,\\[2pt]
\displaystyle
\lim_{r\to\infty}V_k^{(r)}, &
V_k^{(r+1)}=
\min\!\left\{
v_{x,\max},
\sqrt{\frac{a_N^{\max}(V_k^{(r)})}{|\kappa_{p,k}^\star|}}
\right\},
\end{cases}
\label{eq:vlim_iter}
\end{equation}
initialized with $V_k^{(0)}=v_{x,\max}$.  For any candidate speed $V$ at
station $k$, define
\begin{equation}
a_{N,k}(V)=|\kappa_{p,k}^\star|V^2,\qquad
\chi_k(V)=
\left[
1-\left(\frac{a_{N,k}(V)}{a_N^{\max}(V)}\right)^p
\right]_+^{1/p},
\label{eq:ggv_factor}
\end{equation}
where $[\cdot]_+=\max\{0,\cdot\}$ and $p$ is the GGV exponent in
(\ref{eq:ggv}).  The available forward and braking accelerations are
\begin{align}
a_{T,k}^{+}(V)
&=a_T^{+,\max}(V)\chi_k(V)+a_{\rm drag}(V),
\label{eq:fb_accel_available}\\
a_{T,k}^{-}(V)
&=a_T^{-,\max}(V)\chi_k(V)-a_{\rm drag}(V),
\label{eq:fb_brake_available}
\end{align}
where $a_T^{+,\max}$ and $a_T^{-,\max}$ are the calibrated driving and
braking envelopes.  Under the sign convention of~(\ref{eq:drag}),
$a_{\rm drag}$ is usually negative at racing speeds; hence it reduces forward
acceleration and assists braking.

The forward sweep starts from the measured speed,
\begin{equation}
V_0^f=\min\{\max(V_e,V_{\min}),V_{{\rm lim},0}\},
\label{eq:forward_init}
\end{equation}
and propagates
\begin{equation}
V_{k+1}^{f}
=
\operatorname{clip}_{[V_{\min},\,V_{{\rm lim},k+1}]}
\left(
\sqrt{(V_k^f)^2+2\Delta\ell_k\,a_{T,k}^{+}(V_k^f)}
\right),
\quad k=0,\ldots,N-2 .
\label{eq:vfwd}
\end{equation}
The backward sweep is initialized by
\begin{equation}
V_{N-1}^{b}=
\begin{cases}
\min\{V_T,V_{{\rm lim},N-1}\}, & \text{if a terminal speed is enforced},\\
\min\{V_{N-1}^{f},V_{{\rm lim},N-1}\}, & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\label{eq:backward_init}
\end{equation}
and propagates
\begin{equation}
V_{k}^{b}
=
\operatorname{clip}_{[V_{\min},\,V_{{\rm lim},k}]}
\left(
\sqrt{(V_{k+1}^{b})^2+2\Delta\ell_k\,a_{T,k+1}^{-}(V_{k+1}^{b})}
\right),
\quad k=N-2,\ldots,0 .
\label{eq:vbwd}
\end{equation}
The final speed surface is the pointwise feasible envelope
\begin{equation}
V_k^\star=\min\{V_k^f,V_k^b,V_{{\rm lim},k},V_{{\rm cap},t}\}.
\label{eq:vmerge}
\end{equation}
The accelerations stored with the local reference are
\begin{equation}
a_{T,k}^\star=
\frac{(V_{k+1}^\star)^2-(V_k^\star)^2}{2\Delta\ell_k},\qquad
a_{N,k}^\star=\kappa_{p,k}^\star(V_k^\star)^2 .
\label{eq:atan}
\end{equation}
Finally, the time stamps are reconstructed by trapezoidal time integration,
\begin{equation}
\tau_0^r=0,\qquad
\tau_{k+1}^r=\tau_k^r+
\frac{2\Delta\ell_k}{V_k^\star+V_{k+1}^\star+\varepsilon_v},
\label{eq:time_reconstruct}
\end{equation}
and the planner publishes
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}_t=
\{(X_k^r,Y_k^r,\psi_k^r,\kappa_{p,k}^r,V_k^r,\tau_k^r)\}_{k=0}^{N_{\rm out}-1},
\label{eq:reference_tuple}
\end{equation}
after resampling the path, speed and time arrays to the controller cadence.

\section{Model Predictive Contouring Control}\label{sec:controller}
% =====================================================================
The control layer tracks the path-speed reference $\mathcal{R}_t$
from~(\ref{eq:reference_tuple}) by solving a receding-horizon nonlinear
program at the actuation rate $1/T_c$ ($T_c=T_s=0.05\,\mathrm{s}$).
A standard path-tracking MPC penalizes Cartesian deviations
$(x-x^r)^2+(y-y^r)^2$; this couples longitudinal timing to lateral
accuracy and causes aggressive re-plans when the tactical layer shifts
the corridor.  By contrast, the MPCC formulation decomposes the error into
a \emph{contouring} component (normal to the path) and a \emph{lag}
component (tangential to the path), and penalizes only the former.
Longitudinal timing is recovered through a speed-reference term, so a
corridor-side switch only changes the reference sequence without requiring
controller re-initialization.

At control instant $t$ the current vehicle pose defines the local origin.
The state is the planar pose
$\bm{\chi}_k=[x_k,y_k,\theta_k]^\top$
and the input is the commanded speed and front-wheel steering angle
$\bm{\nu}_k=[V_k,\delta_k]^\top$.
The initial condition is
\begin{equation}
\bm{\chi}_{0|t}=\bm{0},\qquad
\bm{\nu}_{-1|t}=
\bigl[V_{-1|t},\;\delta_{-1|t}\bigr]^\top,
\label{eq:mpcc_ic}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{\nu}_{-1|t}$ is the command applied in the previous cycle,
retained for rate-constraint enforcement.
The prediction model is a kinematic bicycle with an identified nonlinear
yaw-rate map,
\begin{equation}
\bm{\chi}_{k+1|t}
=\bm{\chi}_{k|t}+T_c
\begin{bmatrix}
V_{k|t}\cos\theta_{k|t}\\[2pt]
V_{k|t}\sin\theta_{k|t}\\[2pt]
\Omega(V_{k|t},\delta_{k|t})
\end{bmatrix},
\label{eq:mpcc_model}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\Omega(V,\delta)=
\frac{\tan(a_\delta\,\delta+b_\delta)}{L_c}\,
(c_V V+d_V),
\label{eq:yaw_rate_map}
\end{equation}
where $L_c$ is the wheelbase and $\{a_\delta,b_\delta,c_V,d_V\}$ are
identified from closed-loop vehicle data using least-squares regression on
measured yaw rate, speed, and steering angle.  The model is deliberately
low-dimensional: the GGV speed profiler in Sec.~\ref{sec:planner} has
already ensured that the reference speed is dynamically feasible, so the
controller need only reject pose disturbances at the actuation rate
$1/T_c$.

The reference sequence is obtained by transforming the planner output into
the local frame:
\begin{equation}
\bar{\bm{r}}_{k|t}=
\bigl[x_{k}^{r},\;y_{k}^{r},\;\theta_{k}^{r},\;V_{k}^{r},\;
\delta_{k}^{r}\bigr]^\top,
\qquad k=1,\ldots,N_c,
\label{eq:mpcc_ref}
\end{equation}
where the reference steering is reconstructed from the planned path curvature
$\kappa_{p,k}^r$ via the inverse kinematic relation
\begin{equation}
\delta_{k}^{r}=
\arctan\!\left(\frac{L_c\,\kappa_{p,k}^r\,V_k^r}
{V_k^r+\varepsilon_v}\right).
\label{eq:steer_ff}
\end{equation}

For each prediction step, define the position error in the moving
tangent--normal frame of the reference path.  Let
$\bm{t}_k^r=[\cos\theta_k^r,\sin\theta_k^r]^\top$ and
$\bm{n}_k^r=[-\sin\theta_k^r,\cos\theta_k^r]^\top$ be the unit tangent
and normal at step $k$.  The contouring error (normal deviation), lag error
(tangential deviation), heading error, and input-tracking error are
\begin{align}
e_{c,k|t} &=(\bm{n}_{k|t}^{r})^\top
\bigl([x_{k|t}-x_{k}^{r},\;y_{k|t}-y_{k}^{r}]^\top\bigr),
\label{eq:mpcc_ec}\\
e_{\ell,k|t} &=(\bm{t}_{k|t}^{r})^\top
\bigl([x_{k|t}-x_{k}^{r},\;y_{k|t}-y_{k}^{r}]^\top\bigr),
\label{eq:mpcc_el}\\
e_{\psi,k|t} &=\mathrm{wrap}(\theta_{k|t}-\theta_{k}^{r}),
\label{eq:mpcc_epsi}\\
\bm{e}_{\nu,k|t} &=\bm{\nu}_{k|t}-\bm{\nu}_{k}^{r}.
\label{eq:mpcc_enu}
\end{align}
The contouring error $e_c$ measures the lateral deviation from the planned
path and is the primary tracking objective.  The lag error $e_\ell$ is not
penalized in the cost; longitudinal timing is handled implicitly through
the speed reference in $\bm{e}_\nu$.  This choice avoids forcing the
vehicle to chase replanned time-stamps when the tactical layer shifts the
corridor.

The stage cost and the complete optimization problem are
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\min_{\mathcal{U}_t}\;\;&
q_{\Delta\delta}\bigl(\delta_{0|t}-\delta_{-1|t}\bigr)^2
+\sum_{k=0}^{N_c-1}\!
\Bigl[
q_c\, e_{c,k+1|t}^2
+q_\psi\, e_{\psi,k+1|t}^2
+\bm{e}_{\nu,k|t}^\top R_\nu\,\bm{e}_{\nu,k|t}
\Bigr]\\[3pt]
\mathrm{s.t.}\;\;&
\bm{\chi}_{0|t}=\bm{0},\\
&\bm{\chi}_{k+1|t}
=f_d^{\rm kin}(\bm{\chi}_{k|t},\bm{\nu}_{k|t}),
\quad k=0,\ldots,N_c{-}1,\\
&V_{\min}\le V_{k|t}\le v_{x,\max},\\
&|\delta_{k|t}|\le\delta_{\max},\\
&|V_{k|t}-V_{k-1|t}|\le\Delta V_{\max},\\
&|\delta_{k|t}-\delta_{k-1|t}|\le\dot\delta_{\max}\,T_c,
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:mpcc_nlp}
\end{equation}
where $R_\nu=\mathrm{diag}(r_V,r_\delta)$, and
$f_d^{\rm kin}(\cdot)$ denotes the kinematic discrete dynamics
in~(\ref{eq:mpcc_model})--(\ref{eq:yaw_rate_map}).
The weight $q_{\Delta\delta}$ penalizes the first steering increment to
enforce actuator continuity across cycles.  The speed and steering rate
constraints prevent saturation and ensure smooth transitions during
corridor-side switches initiated by the tactical layer.

The NLP is solved in condensed form with $2N_c$ decision variables by a
warm-started interior-point method.  The receding-horizon policy applies
only $(V_{0|t}^\star,\delta_{0|t}^\star)$ and re-solves at the next cycle.
The actuator mapping converts this pair into the physical command vector
\begin{equation}
\bm{u}_{\rm act}=
\mathcal{M}(V_{0|t}^\star,\delta_{0|t}^\star)
=\bigl[T_{\rm cmd},\;P_{b,\rm cmd},\;S_{\rm cmd},\;g_{\rm cmd}\bigr]^\top,
\label{eq:actuator_map}
\end{equation}
where $T_{\rm cmd}\in[0,100]$ is the throttle percentage,
$P_{b,\rm cmd}\in[0,P_{b,\max}]$ is the brake pressure,
$S_{\rm cmd}\in[S_{\min},S_{\max}]$ is the steering actuator position, and
$g_{\rm cmd}\in\{1,\ldots,G\}$ is the gear.  The longitudinal force
request driving the throttle/brake split is
\begin{equation}
F_x^{\rm req}=
\underbrace{c_{d,2}V^2+c_{d,1}V+c_{d,0}
+m\frac{V_{\rm la}-V_{0|t}^\star}{T_{\rm la}}}_{F_{\rm ff}}
+\underbrace{K_p^V(V_{0|t}^\star-V_{\rm meas})
+K_i^V I_V}_{F_{\rm fb}},
\label{eq:force_req}
\end{equation}
and the allocation rule is
\begin{equation}
T_{\rm cmd}=
\begin{cases}
\min\bigl\{100\,F_x^{\rm req}/F_{\max}(\omega_e,g),\;
T_{\rm tc}\bigr\},
& F_x^{\rm req}>0,\\[3pt]
0, & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\label{eq:throttle_map}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
P_{b,\rm cmd}=
\begin{cases}
0, & F_x^{\rm req}>0,\\[3pt]
\mathrm{clip}\!\left(
\dfrac{-F_x^{\rm req}\,r_w}{2k_b},\;0,\;P_{b,\max}\right),
& \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\label{eq:brake_map}
\end{equation}
where $T_{\rm tc}$ is the traction-control limit and $k_b$ converts
pressure to wheel torque.  The steering actuator is mapped as
$S_{\rm cmd}=r_s(\delta_{0|t}^\star+\delta_{\rm off})$
with calibrated ratio $r_s$ and offset $\delta_{\rm off}$.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% =====================================================================
In this paper, a hierarchical game-guided tactical corridor planning and
control architecture is proposed for wheel-to-wheel autonomous racing in
high-speed, highly dynamic adversarial scenarios.  The framework formulates
tactical interaction as a Stackelberg--IBR game whose robust equilibrium is
amortized by a theory-guided Truncated Quantile Critics policy: the game
supplies a structured prior and auxiliary value target, while the TQC
distributional critic suppresses overestimation of collision-prone corridors.
A map-conditioned observation embedding provides curvature and width context,
enabling the policy to generate behavior decisions, overtaking-side selections,
and the parameters of a time-varying Frenet corridor, while a hysteretic
finite-state machine and a deterministic carver convert these into auditable
spatial constraints.  Within
the corridor, a sparse second-order cone program produces curvature-regularized
paths, a forward--backward sweep enforces the GGV handling envelope, and a model
predictive contouring controller executes the reference at the vehicle handling
limit.  By separating tactical intent from physical execution, the learned
policy determines where the vehicle is permitted to drive while the model-based
layers enforce tire limits, actuator bounds, and closed-loop dynamics
in the nominal planner and controller---as empirically validated
in high-fidelity simulation without observed constraint violations---yielding
an adaptive yet interpretable solution for competitive autonomous racing.  Future work will extend the framework to multi-opponent
races with multi-agent game equilibria, incorporate probabilistic opponent
prediction into the corridor constraints, and transfer the approach to
controlled track tests on the Dallara EAV24 platform.
